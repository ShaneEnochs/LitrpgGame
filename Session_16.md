# SESSION 16 — Day 16, Tuesday, November 11, 1862

*Dated archive. Voice §2 — plain modern narrator, period flavor in dialogue only; no metaphor/simile, no "weight"/"quiet"/"honest" as flagged. The build day: morning training (Crane's range, the tinsmith die, Hessler's verdict, Dell's yard), the lift-catch heat-treat with Pearce and the cut-rope demo (held), the three Dunmore arrangements struck, the firm restructured into the green at a parlor meeting, the kids' coats reported near-done, the first full Remedies production run. Closed at the new house — tins setting, records squared, bed. Built from Save Points 1–2.*

## 1. Morning — the stove, then Crane's range

Bill woke to the first hard cold holding in the floorboards, the stove dead, frost on the inside of the kitchen glass. He fed and banked it, warmed the house, and went out into a clear, cold, good-demo morning.

He walked out past the edge of town to Crane's shooting ground and asked plainly for lessons — said he'd barely fired the gun and didn't want to teach himself bad habits. Crane approved the honest approach (the patient answer being the true one and the test both). **Terms set: $0.50/session, own cartridges or his at cost, mornings, weather permitting, no appointment needed.** First session on the spot: grip-and-sight work, dry holds to the tremble, six live rounds (plank twice, four misses). Crane's read — still hands, listens, the rest is hours. Paid **$1**. Bill said he'd bring his own reloads next time; Crane approved and added the **consistency rule** — same load every time so the only variable left is the shooter. **(Doss's training condition: the shooting leg begun in earnest.)**

## 2. The tinsmith — the Quest die collected, lids ordered

At the tinsmith's Bill paid **80 cr** and took the struck *Quest* block-band die — came out true, lettering crisp on the proof lid. He also **placed a lid order**: plain tins by the dozen, cheap, enough for a real production run; the tinsmith to send word in a day or two, settle on collection. **(Quest Remedies production-ready: die owned, makings laid in, base fixed, Arrowood's verbal blessing waiting on a marked tin.)**

## 3. Hessler — the sample-tin verdict (positive, the strongest kind)

At the busy guild counter (rush traffic thick), Hessler gave his verdict on the Day-14 samples. Strong: he'd used the muscle tin on a seized stool, and **his wife used the hand-and-foot tin — her winter-cracked knuckles healed whole in three days, and she wants more.** A washerwoman's hands, not a chemist's opinion. Hessler will move marked tins on the floor; his word carries with crews. Bill told him the die was collected and the line was moving.

## 4. The yard — knife hours with Dell

Bill spent his first sustained hours on blade work with Dell — footwork, cross-draw, plain economical cuts. His Precision hands took the mechanics; Dell rode the telegraphing. **(Doss's training condition: the yard-hours leg begun in earnest. Two of Doss's three named conditions now underway.)**

## 5. Sending for Pearce, the right way

Rather than fetch him (wrong with Pearce, who doesn't surface till midday and won't be summoned) or risk a long wait at the Drovers', Bill sent runner **Eli** with a note that *informed and invited* — metal's cut and read-true, fire's next, he's at the Dunmore warehouse, glad of Pearce's eye when he's about. Peer to peer, no command. Bill went to the warehouse himself to stage the heat-treat. Eli came back at a run; Pearce came behind at his own pace, up and sharp.

## 6. The heat-treat — taught hands-on by a master

Bill had set up fully but **not lit the real heat** — the whole point of the arrangement being Pearce's eye on the fire. Pearce approved that restraint. For the next stretch: Bill's hands, Pearce's eye. Pearce taught the **color reading** (not gauge temperature — the live color of steel at heat), the **case-hardening** in stages (hard skin over tough core), the **quench instant** (the one that can't be undone), and **drawing the temper**. He caught Bill rushing once — *"Wanting it cooks it past. Wait for the color, not your nerves."* Verdict: *"You could do that again without me. Not as well. But you could do it, and know if you'd done it wrong. That's the difference."* The catch came out **hardened true — face-hard, tough-cored, tempered.**

## 7. Fit, trip-test, and the fault the careful look caught

Bill fitted the hardened catch (his build from the start). Trip-tested with a partial load: three controlled drops, three clean bites, dead stops, no slip. Pearce read the bite-marks — holding, marking the drum not the wedge (correct), verbal cert: *"I'd stand under it."*

Then Bill, confident but careful, gave it one more once-over with attention to whether the bite was *straight* — and **caught a real fault: the wedge seated a hair off-square, one face biting deeper.** It had passed the forgiving trip-test but would have crowded the shock to one side under a full hard-cut load with a man beneath — a leaning stop instead of a level one. Caught before the demo, by looking when sure. **(This off-square fault is the session's earned complication — see §16 erratum / the gauge-setback note.)**

## 8. The teaching beat — Cobb and Holt walked through fault and fix

Bill called Cobb and Holt to the frame and taught the lesson plainly off the uneven marks: *the dangerous fault is the one that passes the test and waits; check hardest when you're sure, because confident is when men quit looking.* Then he **walked them through the fix** — Holt on the truing, Cobb reading the marks — fault-to-cause, check-your-own-work. He chose this even though it ran past the 3 PM target. Re-tripped three times after: square and level each time. **(Instruction's strongest beat yet — a live fault on a real machine, taught at the moment of consequence.)**

## 9. The demo — Bill stood under the loaded cage, and it held

Bill asked Pearce for an independent check first — framed as a peer wanting a master's eye, not reassurance. Pearce gave a full independent inspection: seat true, faces even, drum taking the mark, counterweight path clean. *"I'd stand under it. So you can stand under it."*

Then Bill stood under the full cage-load and called for the rope. The worn-through brother cut it clean. The catch bit instantly — one hard crack, **dead-level stop, ~a hand's width of fall, no slip.** Bill stepped out fine. The brother put a hand on the hung cage to be sure it was real: *"It bit. It's yours — the job, the money, and my word to every hauler... You stood under it. Most men would've cut the rope with a dummy."*

**The Dunmore lift-works job: COMPLETE.** Hands sealed it — Pearce first (*"the both of us"* — the courtship's warmest moment), then Cobb and Holt (shaken like tradesmen), then the brother.

## 10. Payouts, and the skill/attribute audit

Bill paid **Pearce 45** (a master's day-fee, honoring the "paid by value after" heat-treat arrangement — its first proof, pitched at the honest top without tipping into charity), and **Cobb 10 + Holt 10** (per-job hand-pay on the build — dignity and money together).

## 11. Settling with the brother — the three arrangements

Pearce left on a good word (*"Send for me when you've got steel that needs reading. I'll be at the Drovers'"* — the door open); the kids sent home high. The brother paid the full **265** in coin, no shaving, and set down a paper he'd written while Bill was teaching the boys. Across the table, all three arrangements struck:

- **Remedies wholesale (rolling).** First batch 30 (15/15) as a test; **6-month price-lock at 15 retail**; his margin steps up every two months — **3 → 4 → 5** (Bill sells at 12 → 11 → 10); after six months the lock lifts, full upside his. First batch due Day 17, ships on the Day-18 run. (His run cadence set as **weekly**.)
- **Gear-maintenance.** Opened at 95 (Bill's-hands rate), walked to **80/mo to the FIRM TREASURY** on the kid-rate framing (the saving passed because the point is lifting the kids — which the brother watched all afternoon); **major repairs billed separately**; Bill keeps training the apprentices at no cost increase. Weekly rounds on wheels/hubs/hinges/brake rigging/the hoist.
- **Storage.** Bill the buyer; made the brother name it. **5 cr/mo, a marked shelf-corner, his men hands-off** — "near throw it in for the trade you're already bringing me." (Keeps Bill's house dull — the exposure line.)

The brother to copy the paper clean for both sides; the demo cage stays hung as his word-of-mouth proof.

## 12. The parlor meeting — the firm restructured into the green

Bill brought **Tatch and Min** to the new house and built, with them, a standard work-for-hire framework — not handed down, hammered out across the table (Bill the customer, they the firm). What came out of the firm's own side:

- **Min:** three job *kinds* written differently — **retainer / per-delivery / one-off**; separate **delivered** and **paid** lines on the books (the gap is where money goes missing).
- **Tatch:** "done" judged by **both** the worker's and the customer's mark, disputes to Tatch; and the **conflict-of-interest guard** he raised himself — *when Bill is the customer, Tatch or Min signs for the firm, not Bill* ("especially when you're being fair, 'cause that's when nobody's checking" — Bill's own discipline handed back).
- **Min:** the **kids' own names on the contracts**, even the small jobs — a record that says they're worth something.

On that frame, the bigger moves:

- **The firm becomes an independent SUBCONTRACTOR of Quest** — its own outfit, own name, Quest its biggest customer; the necks stay separate (protects the children's trust; defensible to Maddox). Tatch valued the independence ("we're not swallowed").
- **Quest pays the firm to distribute Remedies — 1 cr/tin moved**, separate from maintenance and runs, **rising with volume** (tied to Min's count).
- **The union idea** — the firm posts standard rates over its territory (2 cr/run one-off, retainers for steady users), protected by the Compact (Pem won't undercut in-territory). Tatch's framing kept it dull: *fair posted rates, not territorial ownership.* Min's constraint: only promise what the firm can staff (ties union depth to Academy throughput).
- **The mercantile retainer** (25/mo) flagged to move from Bill to the firm — a counterman conversation, re-papered on the form (the retainer follows the labor, now the firm's).

**Min's preliminary read: maintenance 80 + the tin cut (~120/mo at 30 tins/wk) clears the ~210/mo wage bill on standing income alone** — the firm is at or past self-sustaining, pending her real sums. Three recurring Quest/Dunmore lines now: maintenance rounds, distribution per-tin, the runs. **The retail/marketing lessons were PARKED** — the operation is make-and-distribute, not retail; lessons wait until retailing is real. The firm names its own services arm (the whole firm to choose).

## 13. The coats (recovered thread) — near done

Min reported the kids' winter coats (funded Day 12–13, Bill's own money, ~100 cr, eleven kids, bolt-and-sew, youngest-coldest first). As of Day 16: **seven done and worn** — the littles got theirs over the weekend when the hard cold hit — **four remaining** (bigger kids, more wool, 2–3 days out), all eleven inside the week. Ran slightly over (two big coats needed extra wool); Min covered it from the funds, exact figure and change owed once the last sewing hand is paid.

## 14. The stove session — first full Remedies production run

That night, Min and Tatch staying to **watch (framed as learning, not labor — which let Min, charity-averse, accept it freely)**, Bill ran the first full production of the finished line. He worked the fixed wax-and-oil base (the warm-weep defect fixed, recorded ratio, a tunable dial), then split it — comfrey for the **Muscle Balm**, yarrow for the **Hand and Foot Balm** — teaching the why under each step so the kids understood the product they'd distribute. **58 tins** poured clean (~30 of each), set true, no weep:
- 30 for Dunmore (ships Day 18)
- 20 for Hessler (10 each — loading the counter where the goodwill is deepest)
- 8 free samples (4 each) for the firm to place at prospective wholesale accounts (the mercantile among them), each a possible purchase contract on the standard form.

Lids tomorrow at the tinsmith (the firm's first distribution run: tinsmith → Dunmore → Hessler → sample drops). Closed at the new house — tins in their rows, records squared, bed.
