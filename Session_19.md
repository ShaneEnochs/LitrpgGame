## The morning — coffee and the stove

Bill woke to the first hard cold holding and laid the day out in his head before rising: the gunsmith bench. Over coffee at the warmest room in the house, he worked the self-feeding stove design against its one real problem — the gas problem (draft vs. heat-extraction, and the carbon-monoxide risk of a starved, unattended, self-feeding bed).

He started from one rule: **no control on the stove should be able to fully starve the fire or fully rob the chimney of heat.** From that, on paper:

- **Direct-draft bypass** — a lever opens a short, hot, straight firebox-to-chimney route for lighting, morning revival, and banking. The long heat-extraction route only engages once the fire is established and the chimney is already pulling. (This takes the clever passages out of the fight at exactly the two moments — lighting and reviving — where a cold flue would choke a young fire.)
- **Damper and primary-air stops** — the damper closes partway but is physically stopped from shutting fully (a marked iron stop, not an instruction); a fixed primary-air passage through the grate or ash door cannot be closed in normal operation. A badly-set stove wastes fuel rather than producing carbon monoxide.
- **Simplified exhaust** — crossed out the over-clever folded passages; redrew shorter, wider, fewer bends, easy cleaning access. Soot must not slowly choke the draft into danger; the stove that's safe new must stay safe used.
- **Chimney as part of the system** — tall enough to pull, straight where possible, kept hot enough to maintain draft; outside-air intake noted for a tight building so the stove doesn't compete with the room for combustion air.

Verdict in his own head: the design is no longer chasing maximum efficiency. It chases reliability — it **fails toward waste, not toward a roomful of invisible gas.** The dangerous failure is made mechanically impossible, not merely unlikely. Noted to himself: this is now the most *sellable* and the most *attributable* of the bench ideas (a demonstrably safer stove is one people ask who designed) — the same quiet exposure tension every clever thing carries. Not a problem for the morning. The drawing went back in the casing; he did not carry the stove page into another man's shop.

## The gunsmith — the speedloader mule

Bill cleared the top-break by habit and showed it empty before handing it over. The gunsmith (unnamed, Main Stretch; the building partner from the Day-18 supply deal) cleared the bench and they went to the back vise.

They took the **rim-circle off the actual cylinder** — dividers walking the bolt-hole pitch chamber-center to chamber-center, the sixth step closing back on the first; marked the radius. The holder's finger-circle is cut to the real gun, not a number.

Bill settled the real decision before any brass was cut: the spring-brass **fingers** are the trick (too loose dumps cartridges in a pocket; too tight fails the release at the one moment it matters). He asked for the first one made **adjustable to learn from, not pretty** — plain body, dummy rounds only, fingers left proud to work down. He set his priority: **err toward release over grip** (carry it in a stiff pouch so the pouch does the retention the light fingers don't), because a hang over open chambers is worse than a manageable spill risk. The gunsmith agreed to build it that way, making sure it was a decision and not an accident, and added the dimension Bill's speech had already implied: it's not stiff-vs-loose, it's stiff-vs-loose **and all six matched** — they must release *together*, or a five-and-a-hang jams a half-seated round.

They built a **mule** — the throwaway ruler in cheap-gauge brass, fingers proud and a hair too stiff (you can file spring off, never on). Bill ran dummies as each finger came free, strict same-motion testing, failures marked one through six, honest:

- **1, 2** — came clean quickly (the easy ones, not the lesson).
- **3** — held beautifully and **hung** on release; diagnosed (before filing) as a tighter bend radius, more spring in less length — a geometry fault along the whole bend, not a tip-stiffness. Bill's insist-on-diagnosing-first caught it; filing the tip would have weakened it wrong and chased it loose.
- **4** — the day's hard lesson: gripped and dropped clean three or four times, then began letting go **early and inconsistently.** Diagnosis: the cheap spring-brass is **taking a set / fatiguing** — worked and flexed past its happy range, it doesn't fully spring back; no file stroke fixes softened metal. *A spring that works clean day one and gives up its hold by day thirty doesn't bind — it betrays, slow, and a man trusts it right up until it spills his six at the worst time.*
- **5** — died the same way as 4 (answering Bill's question: 4 wasn't an odd flaw, just the first to admit the truth — the gauge fatigues, full stop).
- **6** — over-stiffened on purpose; never spilled, **hung** a third of the time (the other end of the fork; too much spring trades the spill for the hang).
- **1, 2 re-abused** past ~30 cycles softened too — the whole mule tells one story: this brass, this gauge, worked this hard, loses its hold somewhere past two or three dozen draws.

**Bill stopped the holder there** — said it before the want to see a real one standing could argue. He re-ordered the **real build** as orders, not finished dimensions: **better spring temper** (not as a way to hide a bad bend — better metal under a kinder shape, so the metal never works near its edge), **a longer/gentler bend**, and **replaceable fingers on honest seats** (fingers are the wear part; a tired one should be swappable before it lies — the same maintainable instinct as the stove). The gunsmith kept the dead mule and the body-circle/failure marks in the witnesses' corner ("cheapest teacher in the shop"). Goal stated: *not a perfect first drop — the hundredth drop to look like the first.*

## The gunsmith — extractor tune

The cylinder was out and the gun open. Bill named the extractor's fault from a fortnight of cold mornings: the star's lift is a hair lazy and uneven, so on a fouled cold cylinder a case or two stands proud and wants a thumbnail. Three small truths found together: timing (pickup a touch late, doing its lifting in a rush at the end), ragged bearing faces (drag), and the return spring (leave it alone — an extractor that doesn't return fully won't take a full load, a worse failure).

Bill called the order: **smooth first, then timing only if the test still points there** — *timing on a rough bearing is a lie.* The gunsmith stoned the bearing faces smooth (no heroics, not changing the part's character), and the dry dummy test gave the good anticlimax: **smoothing carried most of the cure** — even lift, full return, six clear. The one fault left, isolated honestly with no burr to blame: on a **hard fast break** the pickup still starts a hair late (the cam, not a burr).

Bill **banked it** — left the timing cut in reserve rather than chase the last tenth with other work waiting. *The next proof isn't another file stroke; it's Crane's range with a dirty cylinder.* The gunsmith tagged it and set it with the mule: *pickup late on fast break, no burr left, timing cut held in reserve — the gun gets to vote.*

## Home — the hand-warmer (workable prototype)

Bill's Insight/Intellect read on the Pearce thread first (asked at the range): the books are not the prize to chase; the standing terms are *drink first, books never first, building last*, and the move is to **let the library come on Pearce's clock, never reach for it.** The genuine lever is the **hand-warmer** — the honest, dignified reason to send for a broke ex-assayer over *paid colleague-work he's qualified for*, which inverts the leverage (he has something Bill needs, dignity to a broke expert) and is where trust actually gets built. Caution: don't let the warmer become an obvious Trojan horse for the books either. So: advance the warmer to where Pearce's expertise is genuinely what it needs, *then* send for him over it.

Bill collected materials on the walk home (on the Quest account, at cost, deliberately ordinary buying — a fix-it man's cloth, wax, and assorted iron reads as nothing) and split the evening across the two **bench-solvable** open problems (leaving the iron-*supply* problem, which is sourcing, not bench-work, and which he must not chase loudly).

- **Slow start — SOLVED.** The core was already proven (working ratio, safe band, blister ceiling characterized). Tonight was tuning: surface area is the big lever (finer iron starts faster but burns hotter/shorter toward the ceiling; the fast answer fights the safe answer on the same variable). The answer, against the known safe band: a **fine/coarse iron blend** — a fine fraction to light it fast, a coarse majority to carry a long even sub-ceiling warmth — with the salt/moisture (accelerant) ratio tuned to the edge of the safe band. Result: a **fast, reliable warm-up that stays inside the safe band.** A real solve off his own characterization.
- **Packaging — SOLVED.** Two failure modes pull against each other (the reaction needs air to run, but air before keying means it runs dead in a coat). Architecture (same instinct as the stove and the speedloader): **separate the two jobs.** An **air-tight inner barrier** (waxed-paper / oiled liner, the standing beeswax doing real sealing work) keeps air fully off the charge in storage — inert indefinitely. A **controlled-breathing outer pouch** (woven/perforated to admit air at the rate the reaction wants, tuned against the new blend — too much air pushes the start hot toward the ceiling) meters the air once keyed. **To key:** break/strip the inner barrier; air reaches the charge through the breathing pouch; the blend lights fast and runs even and sub-ceiling. **Fails toward dead-in-storage if mishandled, never toward a blister** — the safety failure designed out, like the stove's invisible gas.

Full keyed bench test passed: strip the inner seal → comes up to useful heat quickly, holds an even sub-ceiling warmth a real working stretch, no early-key, no blister-line spike. **Workable prototype.** The warmer goes from "proven core, three open problems" to **"proven core, two solved, one left."**

**The one open gate: iron supply / scale.** Everything tonight was bench quantities. *Workable* and *producible* differ by sourcing iron (in the right finenesses) past bench scale — a town-and-supply problem, not a bench-tonight one, and one not to be solved loudly (quantity of iron buying reads as a *shape*). This is exactly the **dignified, real reason to send for Pearce** when Bill chooses to.
