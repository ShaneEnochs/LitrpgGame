## The day, scene by scene

### 1. Morning — the new house, first hard freeze
Bill woke to a cold house, stove burned to nothing, frost on the glass and a skin of ice on the basin. Fed the stove, skipped coffee, walked out into it. The day's three named obligations were his to sequence (Marguerite AM, the camp run, Lessard's supper eve), with Day 19 fixed as the gunsmith bench day.

### 2. Marguerite — blade lesson #3 (the trotter joint)
Run on Bill's read, by player request, with his dialogue handled.

Marguerite inspected the belt knife (spine to the light, thumbnail at heel and belly), handed it back without comment — approval. She set him a pork side and the trotter joint and stood back to watch, talking after, not during. Bill read the joint as a hinge: **Perception** found the seam by eye (the dimple over the capsule, the change of bone angle); **Insight/Engineering** read where it *wanted* to open by flexing the foot a few degrees. His standing flaw — wanting to understand the tendon before cutting it, **Intellect** running ahead of the work — he governed and set down, the thing Marguerite has no patience for on a working floor. He witness-marked at the back of the hock by feel, laid the edge into the joint line (not through bone), rolled the foot to open the gap, and took the trotter off in **one clean pass, square cut**. Then two clean shoulder seams, the second faster than the first.

Her verdict: one pass, no miss; last week he'd have talked himself into a second cut he didn't need, and he held off the wanting-to-know and let the knife do the knowing — "that's the whole trade, that part." She walked him to the door (new — she hadn't before). Named the standing gate plainly: he has not cut something that *resists* and pulls back. Different thing; his hands are ready for it when the rest of him is. No coin — work pays the house.

### 3. The home bench — first reload session
Back to the house. Bill laid out the kit and the Day-17 components and loaded the way Crane preaches: scale trued first, charge thrown and trickled to the line, seated, crimped, every round checked to the chamber, a couple pulled and redone for seating depth. **Made exactly 30 cartridges** (five six-shot cylinder-loads) from the ~18 cr of Day-17 components — primers the binding constraint, the thing that runs short first. The better part of two hours, done at his standard, not pushed. Loaded count 0 → 30.

### 4. Crane — the snap-shot lesson (the yard)
Crane read and approved the even, honest loads ("your bench is honest — worth more than you know down a hole"). New direction, the next growth he'd named: first shot from the holster, **speed first, then accuracy** — chasing *first*, not the tight group. Bill's flaw, caught in three draws: building the draw as discrete steps (clear, rotate, level, break) and pausing at level to aim. Crane's fix, walked through by hand: the draw and point are one move; the muzzle finds level *on the rise*, kill the pause. Bill grooved it slow, deliberately under speed, then let it come up — by the back half of the box the ugly high-and-wide flyers had stopped and the first round was landing on or near the mark. Crane: "Better than I figured for a first day… you found the pause and you killed it, mostly, in one morning." Next: bad-start draws (seated, stumbled, off-hand). Box shot down to the last few rounds; brass saved on the ground. **Loaded 30 → ~3.**

### 5. Keller's barrel + Hessler (the cage)
Bought two pickles at Frau Keller's (the established standing bit; she remarked on giving them away). One eaten on the walk, one to Hessler — routine between them, not read into. Hessler accepted it, reported the salve moves (two off the counter, the second buyer came back asking by name; Min has the count).

### 6. Dell — the gun-knife seam (the yard)
Real instructed hours on the seam (the gun-to-knife handoff, not the knife alone), building on Day 17. Dell made the gun "fail" and drilled committing to the knife on the dry click *without looking to confirm* — "your hand counts, not your eyes." Bill's standing **telegraphing** flaw (the shoulder announcing the cross-draw) he worked down measurably, using **Insight** to read his own wind-up. He committed to the knife three or four times without checking — new. Dell: "Better… I had to actually watch for it that last set instead of just knowing." Named the gate both teachers share: what the hand does when the man *means it* — can't be drilled on dowels, and can't be practiced without going somewhere Dell won't send him.

### 7. The throwing-knife question (Dell), and the monster-teacher search (Hessler)
Bill asked Dell whether throwing is worth knowing. Dell's man-fight verdict: a bad trade — you hand your enemy your blade, you'll miss a moving man, it disarms you at the worst moment, and the hours cost is enormous against the seam that keeps the knife *in your hand*. Useful only in a thin slice (snakes, a fleeing target, an already-empty last-ditch, quiet small game). Door open later as a "fun extra," not a survival tool. Dell is the wrong teacher for it regardless.

Player reframe (OOC, banked as Bill's standing read): Bill would throw at **monsters, not men**, which weakens several objections (a beast won't reuse your blade; silence matters underground; some monsters mind a blade in a soft spot more than a pistol ball). Revised read: throwing is a **specialist's third/fourth tool** for a delver — more useful vs. beasts than men, still not a foundation — earning a place only once Bill knows what he's facing below (he doesn't; never past the mouth), carries spares, and has a purpose-made throwing blade.

Bill took this back to Hessler and asked the better question: who teaches *fighting monsters*, not men. Hessler's steer — Crane and Dell are town teachers, neither past a mouth; the real knowers are working delvers (who don't teach) or quitters (who won't relive it). Two leads:
- **The lamp-delver** — a hall name Bill's work has already brushed; deep contracts, came up off something hard, around town more than usual. Knows; won't formally teach. A "buy him a meal, make him talk" prospect (the Crane play).
- **Officer Doss** — a guild **officer** who ran *down* before she ran a desk; the strongest bet for someone who'd actually hand it down. Natural door: Bill already owes her a conversation (the re-measure cover). Hessler's caution: don't ask the whole hall loud — quiet roads.

### 8. The gunsmith — components laid in deep, the quiet way
Down the Main Stretch. Bill bought the full component lot the shop would part with — primers to the bottom of what the gunsmith would sell (primers always the bottleneck on a one-bench frontier shop), powder and lead to match, a little spare brass. **−80 cr**, enough to load ~120–150 rounds. Then proposed a standing weekly supply sized to ~300 rounds/week. The gunsmith read the number cold ("that's a crew's number, or a man planning to be down a hole a great deal") and named the real cost: a named recurring order means a paper trail up the supplier line and a question asked of a small shop ordering five-or-six times its usual primer draw — by the freight agent, the wholesaler, maybe a Bureau man who reads what moves on the rail. He offered the dull alternative himself: **carry it as his shop's increased stock — "my shelf, your cash"** — Bill buys off the deeper shelf for cash as needed, a hair over base, ramping to ~a couple hundred rounds' worth available per week within a few weeks, no recurring slip with Bill's name on it. Bill took it. Caveat banked: keep the buying to this one shop, or the shape shows across town. A bright capability built the quiet way; tomorrow's speedloader now has its supply behind it.

### 9. The home bench again — partial batch, then clean up
Time math: mid-afternoon, the mission a long walk south, the supper the day's one beat to arrive at unhurried and presentable. Not enough daylight to load all 120–150 rounds at standard (4+ hours). Bill ran a clean hour-plus, **made ~35 rounds**, stopped with time in hand. Loaded ~3 → ~38 (full cylinder + ~32 spare). Washed up — powder off the hands, a clean collar — and walked south as the cold came back hard.

### 10. The mission — Father Lessard's supper
The day's spine. (Inside the mission gate Bill is **John**.)

Lessard: older, broad, gray, hands that did real labor before the cloth, eating the same bean soup as the residents — no special plate. He set the first test in thirty seconds: *eat first, don't pitch me.* Bill ate, was easy at a poor table, filled no silence with a pitch. Lessard opened the door himself: Beatrice doesn't spend her credit on many men, so he'd ask plain — what does Bill think the mission can do with him — and warned, "I've buried children this town promised to school and didn't. I'm slow to believe a roof exists before I've stood under it."

**Bill's answer (player-authored, plain register):** what he's seen since arriving — sharp children on the freight lane and the stockpens with nothing between them and a hard short life but missing letters and a trade; the work already done (eleven coats, most sewn and worn; a few young ones on real trades; letters taught off the work itself, a boy reading a repair bill). The philosophy: not one dial but many — a coat, a trade, a steadying hand that guides without owning, and an education — turned a little each. Education is the dial he can't turn alone, and the unclaimed children aren't his to keep. He handed Lessard the *problem* — what would it take, and is this the place — not a finished school, explicitly disclaiming having one to give.

**Lessard's first response:** he believes it (Beatrice is his verifier); singled out the letters-off-the-bill as the tell of a man who's actually watched how such a child learns. Then the cold water, three gates: (1) **sustainability** — a teacher is a forever cost, not a one-time coat; teach a child to expect a door then shut it and you've been crueler than never opening it; (2) **which children** — the firm's own have a roof and trade (the easy ones); the stockpens boys who belong to nobody are who the mission is *for*, and they come with trouble (drink-fathers, a marshal who knows their faces); (3) **the Church layer** — a mission school is the Bishop's, the diocese moves like cold tar and asks why a layman cares this much. Homework set: decide honestly whether this is for the unclaimed boys or only for his own — "two different roofs, and only one is a mission."

**Bill's answer to the homework (player-authored):** refused the versus. It's for all the children or none — sorting them into have-a-littles and have-nothings rebuilds the same fence and calls it charity. He won't pit children against each other; doesn't believe God does, so he won't. The *why*: there are literal monsters in the world now, so the least a man can do is try not to be the other kind; twelve years on we still sort and exploit each other when the resources out of those holes could lift the whole world if we'd come together. He can't change that at scale — one man — but he can change it here, for these children, all of them, and hope it moves someone with more reach to try.

**Lessard's response:** stopped eating. He'd handed Bill a trap (the versus, to sort a man fast) and Bill handed it back — "that's the answer the Gospel gives… you out-Scriptured a priest at his own table." Heard the *why* as a grief, not a pitch ("a man's entitled to keep the wound that made him decent"), and said he believed Bill, which he hadn't expected to. Then the priest's caution, given as a gift: *true is dangerous.* A sincere man who believes the world could be lifted and won't be is the most dangerous kind to those profiting from it staying down; men have been disappeared off this frontier for less than what Bill said over the soup. **Bank the fire; keep the speech for tables where the company's already chosen; let it out where it does work — a coat, a boy's letters, a room with a teacher.** Not be less than he is — how men like him survive long enough to matter. And the verdict, with weight: "all the children, no line — *that* I can carry to a Bishop. *That* is a mission; the other never was." Next supper's agenda, his own naming: numbers, roofs, what the diocese needs, and how a layman stands behind a Church school without either side swallowing the other. "Tonight you've done the thing Beatrice sent you to do. Come back. And eat the soup first again."

Bill thanked Lessard, Agnes, and Beatrice, and walked home in the cold.

### 11. Night — the self-feeding stove (a new bench idea)
Lying in the dark with the stove ticking, Bill turned the cold-at-night problem over with the speedloader's framing: build the fire so it *feeds itself*. He reasoned toward a **gravity-fed coal magazine** — a vertical hopper/tube above the firebox, filled from the top, combustion held low at the *base* of the column, air up through the grate from below, ash to a pan, upper coal warmed-but-not-yet-lit, settling down as the lower layer burns. Plus **longer exhaust passages** routed around the base/sides before the chimney, to soak more heat into the room. (Engineering read: the mechanism is sound and largely correct — this is a real, workable design he reconstructed from first principles. The genuine open problem is **venting/draft safety**: a slow, low-air, banked overnight burn in a sealed-tight house produces a colorless odorless gas that kills in sleep; the longer passages he wants for efficiency *fight* the draft he needs for safety — more bends, more soot, weaker pull. Efficiency vs. draft is the real engineering of the thing.) Filed as a notebook idea — a Quest Instruments candidate, same shape as the hand-warmer/speedloader: a known principle reasoned fresh, build-and-prove on his own bench, with open problems before it's a product (venting first, then the iron work, then coal grade/supply). Not built, not proven — no skill credit yet. Slept.
