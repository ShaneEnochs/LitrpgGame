# DUNGEON MATERIALS — CATALOG (v1)

*Worldbuilding reference for the Frontier Fantasy Solo RPG. Consistent with `game_bible.md` (the canon and tiebreaker, including its voice rules), and the monster-category, orb, aura, and cultivation references. Materials are the physical economy beneath the loot: the metals, bodies, woods, stones, and reagents a dungeon yields, and what the world does with them.*

*Organized by **use**, not rarity — that is how the world treats them. The named stable-enchantment materials (relic steel, deep iron, ghostwood, dragonbone, starfall glass, saint-brass) are established canon and anchor the catalog; the rest extends from them.*

*Format per entry: what it is · source · what it's for · market & law.*

---

## I. Smithing Stock

The materials a cultivator-smith forges into gear beyond ordinary Earth smithing. The reason a high-attribute delver or a durable monster can be hurt at all: ordinary steel fails against both, and these don't.

### Relic steel
A worked metal recovered already-formed from ruins and older dungeon strata — not ore but finished stock, often as broken blades, fittings, or fragments. Takes enchantment safely and holds an edge against things that turn ordinary steel. One of the few materials a firearm can be built from without cracking under infusion. Reforged rather than smelted from scratch.

*Source:* buried ruins, sealed dungeon strata, old first-wave sites.
*For:* enchanted blades, armor, and the frames of stable rune-guns.
*Market & law:* expensive and sought; recovery from sealed sites is regulated, and worked relic steel of unknown provenance draws USBD questions.

### Deep iron
A dense, dark metal pulled from the deepest worked levels of mountain dungeons and old mining sites. Heavier and tougher than any natural iron, it resists heat and force that would deform steel, and it accepts runework along its grain. The structural material of serious enchanted gear.

*Source:* deep dungeon levels, the Cinder Range and similar mining sites.
*For:* armor, heavy weapons, rune-gun barrels and actions, warded seal-works.
*Market & law:* a real commodity with a real market; contested claims and guild ownership are common. Legal to trade, costly to obtain.

### Dragonbone
Bone and analogous hard tissue from the largest and most powerful Beasts and Giants — not literally from dragons, but named for the scale of what it comes off. Lighter than metal, nearly as strong, and it takes enchantment with unusual willingness. Prized for gear that must be both strong and light.

*Source:* the bodies of the strongest Beasts, Giants, and the rare apex monster.
*For:* light enchanted weapons and armor, rune-gun components, focuses.
*Market & law:* rare, because what it comes off is rare and lethal to harvest. High value; the body of a Giant or stronger is often claimed before a small crew can profit.

### Saint-brass
A warm-toned alloy associated with consecration and Church work, recovered from certain sites and refined under guild and Church oversight. It carries holy and binding runework better than any other common material, and resists the corruption that Spirit, Undead, and Infernal essence works on ordinary metal.

*Source:* specific dungeon sites; refined under oversight.
*For:* consecrated weapons, exorcists' gear, binding-work, stable rune-guns intended against the three Church categories.
*Market & law:* trade runs through or near the Church; unsanctioned dealing in saint-brass attracts both USBD and ecclesiastical attention.

### Construct-plate
Material salvaged from the bodies of Constructs — stone, metal, or stranger stock already shaped by whatever made the Construct. Often usable with less reforging than raw stock, since it comes out durable and sometimes already half-worked.

*Source:* Construct bodies.
*For:* armor and structural gear; the most reliable smithing windfall, since a Construct fights to destruction and leaves its whole body behind.
*Market & law:* legal, valued, and steady. The category a working crew most counts on for a payday.

### Beast hide, fang, and sinew
The workable parts of Beast bodies — hide that turns blades, fang and claw for points and edges, sinew for binding and draw. The broad base of the materials economy, ranging from near-worthless vermin to the prized hide of an apex predator.

*Source:* Beast bodies, the most common kill in the world.
*For:* armor, leatherwork, bowstrings, hafts, and a thousand ordinary uses; the better grades take light enchantment.
*Market & law:* fully legal, openly traded at any cage. Cheap at the low end, genuinely valuable at the apex.

## II. Reagents

The alchemical economy, drawn mostly from Plant, Fungal, and Ooze drops. Poisons, sedatives, restoratives, and the curatives that come from the same things that sicken. In public a careful person calls all of it "medicines."

### Plant reagents
Sap, thorn, pollen, fruit, and root from dungeon Plants — the richest single source of alchemical stock. Yields sedatives, paralytics, poisons, and genuine restoratives depending on the Plant and the preparation.

*Source:* Plant-category monsters and Plant-themed sites; the Verdant Wilds and ghostwood country are reagent-rich.
*For:* medicine, poison, sedation, the alchemist's whole trade.
*Market & law:* the restorative and common grades trade openly as "medicines." Anything that paralyzes or poisons crosses into restricted territory and is sold on the side.

### Fungal reagents
Spores, fruiting bodies, and the threads of dungeon Fungal colonies. The most dangerous reagents to harvest and among the most potent — the same spore that sickens a lung yields, prepared right, a powerful curative.

*Source:* Fungal-category monsters and spore-heavy sites.
*For:* potent curatives, hallucinogens, sedatives, and a few things that don't have polite names.
*Market & law:* high-value and tightly watched; the line between medicine and weapon is thin enough that Fungal stock draws scrutiny. Harvest is hazardous enough to command a premium.

### Ooze residue
The settled or rendered remains of an Ooze — corrosive concentrates, solvents, and rare stable residues left once the body stops digesting. Ugly to harvest, since the material destroys what touches it until it's neutralized.

*Source:* Ooze-category monsters.
*For:* solvents, etching and refining agents (including in rune and smith work), and a few caustic weapons.
*Market & law:* legal but specialized; the corrosive concentrates edge toward restricted when weaponized. Sold to crafters and refiners more than to chemists.

### Venom and gland stock
Toxin harvested from venomous Beasts and Aberrations, kept in the gland or stabilized. Potent, specific, and dangerous to handle; the base of the better poisons and of certain medicines in small doses.

*Source:* venomous Beasts, some Aberrations.
*For:* poisons, paralytics, and — diluted — pain medicine and heart treatments.
*Market & law:* **restricted** in any weaponizable quantity; the legitimate trade runs through licensed apothecaries and demands paperwork. The quiet trade is brisk and illegal.

## III. Essence Materials

The refueling economy and the open industrial question. Per canon: essence stones refuel and refresh a cultivator's core but never grow it, and the technology to use them as industrial power does not yet exist in 1862.

### Essence stones
Crystallized essence recovered from dungeons and from the bodies of stronger monsters. A cultivator can absorb one to refill a spent core or hold a reserve; they range from faint chips to dense stones off apex creatures. They do not grow a core — only refuel it — and forcing raw essence at a core to make it grow risks rupture.

*Source:* dungeon strata and the bodies of stronger monsters across every category.
*For:* refueling cultivators; reserve and trade; the presumed future of industrial power.
*Market & law:* a real and liquid commodity, openly traded and assayed by grade. The largest and purest are strategic goods, and the powers watch their movement.

### Grade and character
Stones vary in two ways the market cares about: **density** (how much essence, from a faint chip to a strategic stone) and **character** (the flavor of the essence — a grave-cold Undead stone, a burning Infernal stone, a clean Beast stone). Character matters to rune-workers and alchemists who want essence of a particular kind, and to the Church, which demands oversight of stones off the three forbidden categories.

*Source:* as above; character follows the source category.
*For:* refueling (any), and specialized rune/alchemy/consecration work (specific character).
*Market & law:* clean-character stones trade freely; Spirit, Undead, and Infernal stones are restricted-adjacent and Church-watched. A burning Infernal stone is not something to assay openly.

### Mana-conductive stock
Rare stones and metals that carry or store mana rather than essence — the materials that let a focus, a rune-gun, or a seal-work hold and channel a charge. Distinct from essence stones, which hold the core's fuel; these hold the spell's fuel.

*Source:* specific dungeon sites, often the same strata as relic steel and starfall glass.
*For:* focuses, rune-gun mana channels, seal-works, anything that must store or move mana.
*Market & law:* valuable and specialized, of keen interest to rune-workers and the USBD's certification arm. Legal, watched, and not cheap.

## IV. Enchantment & Rune Stock

The materials that hold worked runes and stable enchantment — the physical substrate of the closely guarded rune discipline. These are the materials the secret is *about*: the rune knowledge is protected, and these are what that knowledge is applied to.

### Ghostwood
A rare, pale wood from the deep forests — the Verdant Wilds and ghostwood country — that holds stable enchantment better than nearly anything and resists the instability that wrecks ordinary materials under infusion. One of the few things a firearm can incorporate without cracking when infused.

*Source:* deep old forest, harvested rather than dungeon-dropped; rare and slow-growing.
*For:* wands, hafts, focuses, rune-gun stocks and grips, stable enchanted gear.
*Market & law:* rare and expensive; harvest is contested and the trade is watched. Worked ghostwood of good grade is a small fortune.

### Starfall glass
A glassy material from the Redglass Basin, left by whatever fell there, prized for holding fine and intricate runework that other materials can't carry. Behaves oddly during the Basin's mana storms — magic worked on or near it goes unpredictable when the glass storms blow.

*Source:* the Redglass Basin badlands; gathered, dangerous to reach.
*For:* fine runework, lenses and focuses, precise mana-channeling components, rune-gun sights and channels.
*Market & law:* valuable and regionally specific; the Basin's outlaw country makes supply unreliable. Legal, but a starfall-glass focus marks its owner as someone who deals in serious rune work.

### Rune-blank stock
The general class of materials prepared specifically to *receive* runes — relic steel, deep iron, ghostwood, starfall glass, saint-brass, and dragonbone all serve, each suited to different work. A rune-worker chooses the blank for the rune: saint-brass for binding and consecration, deep iron for durability, ghostwood for stability, starfall glass for fine detail.

*Source:* as each named material above.
*For:* the substrate of all rune and stable-enchantment work; the material half of a discipline whose knowledge is the guarded half.
*Market & law:* the materials are tradeable; the *knowledge* of how to work them is the closely held secret, guild- and individually-guarded. A person can own the blank legally and still be unable, or unlicensed, to rune it.

### Corruption-resistant stock
The subset — saint-brass above all, certain deep-iron grades, some clean-character stones — that resists the way Spirit, Undead, and Infernal essence rots and turns ordinary materials. Essential for any gear meant to be used against the three Church categories, since lesser materials degrade or turn in their presence.

*Source:* as the named materials; saint-brass is the standard.
*For:* exorcists' gear, consecrated weapons, binding-work, anything carried against the forbidden categories.
*Market & law:* trade overlaps Church oversight; the best of it moves through sanctioned channels. The category where the USBD and the Church most directly share an interest.

## V. Finished Instruments & Devices

Not raw stock but *made objects* — the working gear a delver, rune-worker, or crafter carries, built from the materials above and worth more than the sum of them. The world has had twelve years to turn dungeon materials into tools, and a small trade of makers (mostly back east and in Saint Orison) produces instruments that do what no ordinary Earth device can. They are **analog, never numeric** — needles, dials, graduated arcs, colored fluids, tuned tone — consistent with the no-screens rule: an instrument *indicates*, a person *reads* it. None of them displays a number on its own; reading one is a skill.

A finished essence- or mana-instrument has two halves, and the distinction is the whole of how it's serviced, sold, and broken:
- **The body** — case, movement, needle, dial, stem, glass, springs, jeweling, the ordinary mechanism that turns what's sensed into something a person can read. This is *machinery*. A good tinker who has never seen one can still reason about a fouled body, because a binding needle is a binding needle whatever it's pointing at.
- **The sensing element** — the dungeon-material part that actually feels essence or mana (a mana-conductive sliver, a rune-scribed bezel, a clean-character stone seated at the heart of it). This is the part that requires the guarded knowledge to make, tune, or repair, and the part an unlicensed hand must not touch. It is also the part that can be *fouled* in ways no mechanism explains.

### Essence gauge ("density glass," "the needle")
The anchor instrument: a pocket device, watch-sized but heavier, that reads how thick or thin ambient essence runs. A graduated arc and a dark needle under a glass; a stem to zero it; a bezel scribed with fine calibration runes. A delver uses it to tell strong ground from thin, to confirm a hole hasn't gone wrong, and — most valuable — to catch the kind of strange where the air tests sound but the *essence* is being drawn down, which a man can't sense unaided until it's too late. (This is exactly the divergence Bill measured at Hollow Reach with flame and gas; the gauge does it in a pocket, and far more finely.)

*Source:* made instruments, not dungeon drops — built by specialist makers from a mana-conductive or clean-stone sensing element, a rune-scribed bezel, and an ordinary precision movement. The good ones come from the East or Saint Orison.
*For:* delver safety and prospecting; the single most useful instrument a careful crew carries.
*Market & law:* expensive, legal to own, and watched in the way all essence-gear is. The *sensing element* and bezel-runes are licensed rune-work; a person may own the gauge and be wholly unable (or unlicensed) to repair its heart. Bodies can be serviced by any good hand.

### How essence-instruments foul (the body/sensing split in practice)
A gauge that "won't read true" has its trouble in one half or the other, and telling which is the entire diagnostic question:
- **Body faults (a good tinker's work):** a needle binding on a bent pivot or grit; a movement knocked out of true by a fall; a fouled or sticking stem; condensation or oil under the glass; a weak return spring letting the needle drift or stick high then drop. These present as *mechanical* symptoms — a tinker can reason them out the way Bill read the lamp's creeping wick, by mechanism and feel, without understanding essence at all.
- **Sensing faults (licensed rune-work, not a tinker's):** the sensing element itself *corrupted, saturated, or detuned* by what it was exposed to — most often a strong or strange essence environment that overloads or "stains" it, after which it reads fat where there's nothing and dead where there should be a reading, and **won't settle.** The bezel-runes can also be scrambled or dimmed. No mechanism is wrong; the heart is. This is past any tinker and is the licensed maker's or rune-worker's repair — or a replacement element.
- **The honest trap:** the two can *look identical* from the outside (both "won't read true"), which is why a careful tradesman opens the body, rules the mechanism in or out, and refuses to touch the sensing element. A body fault that mimics a sensing fault (e.g. a needle binding so it reads erratic) is a tinker's win and a cheap save; a true sensing fault closed back up honestly is worth more to the owner than a bluffed "repair" that fouls it further.

### Other finished instruments (extend as play needs)
- **Mana-meter / charge-glass:** reads a focus's or rune-gun's stored mana charge rather than ambient essence; same two-half construction.
- **Resonance tuners and focuses:** rune-worker's bench instruments for tuning enchantment; starfall-glass or ghostwood bodied.
- **Safety lamps as instruments:** the ordinary gauze lamp doubles as a crude essence indicator, since flame burns partly on ambient essence and goes blue-cored and short where essence thins (`game_bible.md` §13). A *purpose-built* essence-reading lamp — flame height against a marked scale — is a plausible, buildable middle ground between a plain lamp and a true gauge, and a thing a clever unawakened engineer could approach from the mechanical side without licensed rune-work. (Noted as a live design idea in play.)

*Market & law for finished instruments generally:* the bodies are ordinary machinery and freely serviced; the sensing elements and any runework are licensed, watched, and a USBD-certification matter. Provenance applies — an instrument of unknown make invites the same questions a worked metal does.

## VI. Crafted Sundries (domestic durables)

Apart from the instruments and the weapons, the largest consumer trade in dungeon materials is in plain household goods — runework bound into common materials to do a useful domestic thing, indefinitely. They are **luxury durables**, not tools of a trade: an enchanted lantern that never needs oil, a stove that heats itself, a box that keeps food cold, a bedstone that holds warmth through a winter night, a razor that never dulls. The defining trait is that they **run on stored or ambient essence, not the user's mana**, so an ordinary unawakened household can own and use them with no core at all — which is exactly why the market for them dwarfs the market for orbs or tomes.

- **What makes them work:** simple, stable runework — the easy, legal end of the craft, closer to the certified warder's and transmuter's trades than to combat enchantment — bound into ordinary or lightly dungeon-derived materials, drawing a trickle of ambient essence or running off a seated essence chip that is recharged or swapped like a battery. The effect is fixed at making and cannot be releveled; a broken one is repaired by re-inscription, not by the owner.
- **Who makes and sells them:** licensed enchanters and crafting-class makers, mostly back east and in Saint Orison, where the buyers, the essence stock, and the certified runework all sit together. The goods are assessed, taxed, and registrable like any dungeon product (`game_bible.md` §14).
- **The frontier picture:** the materials are western, the workshops and the customers are eastern, so most sundries are made from western loot and shipped back east to be sold. Frontier stock is thin, late, and marked up — a self-heating stove or an enchanted lantern is a wealthy household's purchase out here, and a working family does without (`game_bible.md` §17; price anchors in `campaign_state.md`).
- **Use-requirements:** the domestic ones carry little or none — that is the point. The line blurs only where a sundry does something a person could turn to harm or that the law watches (a warming effect strong enough to burn, a cold-box large enough to be a contraband cache); those pick up the registration and occasionally the floors that gear and orbs carry (`ability_framework_v1.md` §8).

## Notes on the materials economy

- **Why this layer exists at all:** ordinary steel and lead fail against durable monsters and high-attribute cultivators, and ordinary materials crack under enchantment. The whole materials economy is the world's answer to both problems — and the reason blades came back, since a blade takes enchantment safely and a gun mostly doesn't.
- **The harvest problem:** the best materials come off the most dangerous monsters, and a small crew often can't hold a Giant's or apex body long enough to profit. Material wealth and survival pull against each other.
- **The provenance problem:** worked materials of unknown origin — relic steel, saint-brass, Infernal-character stones — invite the question of where they came from, which is a USBD and sometimes a Church question. Clean provenance is itself worth money.
- **Cross-references:** monster-category drops (`monster_categories_v1.md`) name which category yields which material; the stable-enchantment list ties to the firearms-vs-enchantment rule in `game_bible.md` §15; rune knowledge itself is guarded canon and only partly catalogued here.
- **Finished instruments (§V):** the made-object layer above the raw stock — essence gauges, mana-meters, focuses, instrument-lamps — built with a serviceable mechanical *body* and a licensed essence/mana *sensing element*. The body/sensing split governs who may repair what, and is the diagnostic question when one "won't read true."
- **Crafted sundries (§VI):** the consumer layer — household durables (lanterns, stoves, cold-boxes, bedstones, razors) that run on stored or ambient essence and need no core. The largest legal trade in dungeon materials by volume, mostly made from western loot and sold east, which leaves frontier stock thin and dear.
