# Session 11 — Day 11, Thursday, November 6, 1862

*Write-once archive. Late autumn, the first hard cold set in. New Abilene. Bill begins the day at Maddox House with 67 cr in pocket, 18 owed on the Quest tab, the firm's 60 still moving east, and a stacked agenda to sequence.*

---

## The day in order

**Dawn — the Garrick yard.** Bill went down before light to see Hollis's rebuilt rig off in the Thursday freight string and make sure the work held under load. The string was half-formed, lamps still lit on the lead wagons — caught in the window between formed-up and rolling. Hollis greeted him at the rig; Bill said plainly he'd come only to see a friend off, no business in it. The line landed on Hollis (a working man handed a kindness with no invoice) — "You're an odd one, Wyatt. Good odd." Tom had done the back spring pack himself; it held when Hollis rocked her loaded the night before. Hollis settled the payment terms openly: Min held the paper, he'd authorize the **+200** the moment the string cleared the gate rolling clean. The rig rolled out, off-front hub running true, no wobble, no heat-shimmer — **the wheel held.** Hollis: Cinder road and back, three days if the pass behaves; when he's in, Bill and Tom check the back hub and he buys the coffee.

**Min, at the yard.** Min had watched the roll for the same reason Bill had — the paper doesn't get thumbed until the wheel rolls clean. She reported it witnessed (a true log: she saw it, didn't just hear it). Bill instructed: settle the firm's two debts (28 the book + 60 the wire bridge) **against** the 200, marked pending until cash in hand, cleared the day it lands. On the **wool/coats**: Min had checked Tatch's sums and found the hole herself — Yost's used coats ~30 each in a lot; raw fleece is cheap but raw isn't a coat (carding, spinning, weaving/fulling, *then* a sewing-hand), and a depot sewing-hand sews, she doesn't spin. The in-between work is unpriced; the wool idea only beats Yost if that's cheap or if they buy made cloth and skip half of it. Bill: price the off-fleece work and the made cloth/sewing both, and read **Dunphy** for a lever (find where he hurts, promise nothing — a man getting something he needs sells cheaper). Fast, because of the cold. He also told Min to send a kid to Keller's for two pickles — eat one as the honest errand, carry the other plus a folded word to **Hessler**: Bill is taking **Bly** up on his offer, pass it along. (Min took the fold unread — she doesn't read what isn't hers.) She read Bill for whether the cold was making a decision for him; he said he was carrying a lot but things were starting to settle. Bess sent up to the cage.

**Money settled (the firm income against the firm debts).** The 200 cleared through Min: 88 off it (28 + 60), 112 to the firm treasury. Bill drew **100** from the firm to his pocket for the day's costs (a draw, noted, not a gift). One true running figure set: **pocket 167, firm treasury ~322, the two firm debts cleared.**

**Calloway's (first visit) — the trust + the letter.** Mrs. Maddox was inside reading the trust line by line; she'd found the named-succession clause herself and made Calloway explain it before passing it. She'd sign because the instrument was clean, not because Bill charmed her — and she'd told Calloway to his face she'd walk the day it stopped being clean. Bill handed Calloway the **sealed letter to Malcolm** (the warm half, signed Q), to ride east behind privilege after the wire, no Weaver name on the outside of anything; reply by runner the hour it lands. Bill weighed staying to witness the signing and chose to leave before she finished — the play that works over the play that feels good (a Maddox guards against being managed; his face at the table tips toward "managed"). Left with "tell her hello and thank you, nothing she has to answer." Calloway: "She'll know what it means that you didn't stay. Maybe not today. She'll know."

**The slaughterhouse — Marguerite, blade lesson #2 (the trotter).** *(Run on Bill's read, by request.)* Bill led with the engineer's frame — show me where the joint *wants* to come apart — and worked it like a hinge, witness-marking by feel before committing, taking her one correction without argument. One friction beat: his urge to *understand* the tendon ran ahead of just cutting it; he read her limited patience for theory on a working floor and backed off. **No second miss.** Marguerite warmed a notch (this one learns, doesn't waste her floor). **Melee (Blades)** advanced from barely-Novice toward solid Novice — stance, grip, seam anatomy, basic joint work, real blade-on-material done. **Lesson #3 scheduled Day 18 (Thu, Nov 13), morning** — same day as Lessard's supper (evening); they don't collide, but Day 18 is now loaded.

**The mercantile — retainer round, tab settled, Charlie's first lesson.** Bill settled the **18-cr Quest tab in cash** so the company account opens clean, not on a carried debt (pocket 167 → 149). Tab now 0; the **+25/mo retainer begins.** He asked the counterman's leave to teach the nephew the bench rounds (check before it breaks; the three categories do-now/watch/leave), writing every step down so the reading rides on the work — a bill is just another thing to read. The counterman liked it (his condition was "read a bill"; Bill handed him the trade-eye and the letters off one hour, one retainer). The nephew — **named this session: Charlie, ~9–10** — took his first reading lesson (bench-part names, letter-to-sound matching started) and began the rounds method. New teaching-ledger line.

**Calloway's (second visit) — the title search.** Bill set the search on **Pearce's building** (−50; pocket 149 → 99). Scope: the exact lien figure, years owed, compounding, and the date it tips to a tax sale, plus the public plat (lot lines, footprint). Calloway drew the line on the *interior* — a plat is public, but a man asking the inside of a building he doesn't own reads as "studying a house," not buyer's diligence; the clean road to the rooms is Pearce walking Bill through them when the season's come. Calloway gave a townsman's-and-old-professional's sense of it: stone front (the fire point — matters for hot work), deep lot, the serious flue-room at the rear, living quarters upstairs, a cellar almost no one's seen. Numbers a few days out by runner.

**The Clarion.** Bill bought a paper (~2). Lead: the Bureau's classification standard wired to the guilds, with the local hook — **Hollow Reach reclassified a "Special Delve,"** written so a windfall-hungry reader fills in *treasure*; it'll pull other guilds, fortune-seekers, and the curious toward the worst ground in the territory, and it's a fresh turn of the screw on Doss in public. Also: the cheap-wire item as settled fact (the Combine's to price as the rail is); a Bureau reporting-circular (recovered material to a licensed assessor and the register before sale/transport — touches Quest Instruments someday); Saint Orison backlog/circuit talk. Nothing on Bill, the firm, the mission, Rusk, the land company, or his father. No Hollow Reach contract on the board — writ business, handled above the hall floor; its absence is its own fact.

**The guild hall — the board, Hessler, the repairs.** Bill went up to read the board and say hello. Contracts now carry the Bureau tier/grade marks (he can half-read them after Bly schooled him); ordinary postings (caravan/escort, close local Beast bounties, ruins/survey); the Day-10 crew-call still posted and marked up — *wanted fast, wanted experienced*, sitting longer than a guild likes. No Hollow Reach posting. **Hessler** was mid-line; Bill waved, got the two-finger acknowledgment, came back when it cleared. Hessler took the pickle (and the folded word, filed, not narrated back across an open counter). On the categories, from the clerk's side: the marks are official but imperfect — set by eastern men who never stood in the sites, some sit wrong to anyone who knows the ground, and the Special box has his counter busy with the wrong questions (men asking after a hole that's never paid a copper). On the work: the lamp-delver collected his lamp (better than new, told two men); pieces left for "Quest" — a movement that won't wind, a case spring, and a third (a fine gauge-family body). **Three body-only repairs, done in the open at the counter** (deliberate "known for the work" posture, line held visibly):
- *Movement* — gummed mainspring (varnished oil), cleaned and re-oiled; an ordinary pocket movement, no heart in it. Body fault.
- *Case spring* — re-tensioned. Trivial.
- *Fine gauge-family piece* — read conservatively from the outside first, notebook out: the needle binding read as a *catch* (mechanical) not a won't-settle (heart). Opened the body carefully, traced it to a weak return spring + grit, freed the needle to swing true. **Did not work the sensing element** (the fault was never there). Caveat given and noted: body fixed, needle moves true mechanically, but the owner should confirm it *reads* true against known ground — whether the heart is honest is a maker's question. Hessler: "Quest fixed the bodies, the hearts are the owners' own to prove. That's the part'll make 'em trust it."

**Diagnostic note (Bill's, recorded).** Bill has now seen the gauge family fail in both halves — Bly's (body sound, heart fouled, a new wrong cast) and today's (heart untouched, body faulted) — and logged the *difference in how the needle misbehaved*: a **catch/snag = mechanical**, a **won't-settle = heart**. Two data points, marked as such, not a rule. Today's heart he *saw* (a glance, confirming it's a true gauge) but did not examine.

**The training yard — Dell.** Slack-hour, the morning musters gone. Another man was drilling a knife alone at the far end. Bill took a wooden practice knife and copied his movements while pretending not to — wanting to be *caught* rather than ask. His hands aped the shapes (Precision); the footwork underneath gave him away as the beginner he is. The man let it ride, then crossed over. **Dell** — frontier knife-fighter, late twenties, fights for a living, one name only. Read Bill's drilling exactly ("your hands are fine, everything under 'em's wrong"). Bill owned the not-asking as the real fault, introduced himself as Wyatt, offered to pay and was honest he hadn't much (a tinker). Dell waved off the money ("I'm not a teacher for hire — there's Crane"), gave a few minutes free because teaching tests his own edge. **Taught:** the base (feet, weight, standing to stay standing when shoved), the close grip vs. the slaughterhouse grip, and the core idea — *position before the cut*, get where the thing can't easily hit you while you can hit it. On the gun question, freely: the room picks the weapon (tight dark = blade, no reload, no muzzle-flash; open/range = gun); smart hands carry both. On the supervised-delve road, independently confirming Hessler and Bly: it's *being known* to a small-crew man, not asking a boss cold; get more lessons and footwork in first — a boss can smell a man who'll freeze. A door left open the width a stranger earns. **Bill drilled it ~an hour solo** afterward, writing the footwork into the working notebook as engineering — the physics of the base, center of mass, force-in-and-answer — building reps a frame to hang on. **Melee (Blades)** firmed to solid Novice with a real foundation (feet under the hands at last). No rounds burned (the revolver never came out).

**The gunsmith.** Bill went for cartridge pricing and the lay of the shop. Established prices: revolver cartridges ~30–40/box of fifty; reloading the cheaper long game. Services: ordinary repair, cleaning, sight/spring/action work, rebarreling, stocks the reloading makings, registration, used trade. Honest that infused/rune-firearm work isn't his bench (a specialist's, mostly Saint Orison or east) — the where-my-knowledge-stops honesty Bill respects. Bill asked about **teaching reloading** so he doesn't hurt himself; the gunsmith will walk a first-loads session with a man who buys the kit (Bill's Explosives background means the powder *respect* is already there). **Kit pricing set:** tooling (press, dies, measure, scale, priming tool, sundries) ~120–160; starting consumables ~30–50; all-in ~150–210 by grade; per-round well below factory after. Bill floated trading **handloads** for a discount; the gunsmith declined (he can't vouch another man's reloads to his customers — *trust the round* is what he sells) but **counter-offered labor-for-discount**: a day of competent bench work for real money off the kit. Bill took it, with his own rider — start the **rough work tonight** (cleaning, breakdown, degrease) in failing light, leave the **fine fitting work for tomorrow afternoon fresh**. The gunsmith liked it ("that's the right way round"). **The discount amount was not pinned** — left for tomorrow afternoon when both know the stack and the quality. Bench relationship planted (gunsmith unnamed). **Side benefit logged:** Bill had never had firearms *apart* before — the rough stack across types (revolvers, breech-loaders, lever-actions) gave him real hands-on **Engineering** growth on firearm mechanism (a first foundation under Shooting too); he sketched the unfamiliar actions in the working notebook.

**Evening — the Drovers', a second pass at Pearce.** *(Played out.)* Bill read the room (ordinary tells only — unawakened, no pressure to feel): teamsters and freight men plain, a knot of two-three reading delver (armed for use, apart, watching the door), Pearce at the bar-end with plain rye. Bill took a stool a seat down, ordered, gave a friendly-not-warm greeting — bought *ordinariness*, no hook. He told Fenner "I'll have what he's having" (rye, 25; pocket ~97 → ~72) — a sideways signal of sameness, not charity (buying Pearce a drink would trip the pride wire; Pearce refuses finer drinks bought for him). Then Bill worked his notebook in view — first the firearm page (making himself furniture), then the instrument page (the assayer's-world lure), flipping so it never read as fishing; Pearce's eye drifted to it by his own choice. Bill lingered on the instrument page, wrote a real underlined question big enough to read at a lean (the truest one from the day — does a stained sensing element ever read true again, or is it dead once a bad site gets into it). Pearce leaned, read it, *wanted* to answer — and his pride won; he turned his glass a quarter and let it go. Not a no: he didn't move away, didn't close off. Then Bill made small talk about the cold and a coat (he'd misjudged the west for desert, needs proper wool) — the zero-stakes subject Pearce *would* talk about, and did: grumbled, steered Bill on where to actually buy wool, and clocked the word *tinkering* without Bill selling it. Bill left on the good note — friendly nod, no lingering — banking a clean second pass: ordinariness, his eye drawn to the work by his own choice, a true question wanted-but-unanswered, and Pearce *talking to him* instead of past him. Deposit made; the season's long.

**Night — Maddox House, notebooks and bed.** Walking home Bill had an idea off the sight of the firm's kids running errands: a **runner-cart** (a kick-scooter — deck, upright, basket low and close to the axle, a brake for the slope; a standardized kid-maintainable fleet would multiply the firm's reach, but a child on a wheeled cart is *remarkable* where a running kid is invisible — cuts against the forgettable posture). That opened the bigger problem — the town's roads turn to mud — and the Roman insight (it's drainage and crown before surface; bed it on stone, crown it, drain it). Filed honestly as *politics before engineering* — a road is the council's business, not a thing a dull-and-rooted tinker raises loud; maybe the firm's own most-run route someday, never civic first. That in turn opened the *materials* angle, which is genuinely Bill's: a **road-patch chemistry** — cheap quick-set, a summer water-pulling powder and a winter water-mixed set, carried in a sack and laid where a road eats wagons (Hollis's rig, the bog Petey was taught). Marked an idea-to-consider, gated on reagents and chemistry training he doesn't have yet (Novice; the town's reagents dry). Then 30 minutes with the medical notebooks (for Friday's kitchen work) and the notebooks updated. Bed.
