# GAME BIBLE — Frontier Fantasy Solo RPG (current canon)

*This is the authoritative, current ruleset and setting for the campaign — the parts that change only when a rule or the world is deliberately changed, never as part of normal play. The session machinery (start/close protocols, the file manifest, the glossary, the content index) lives in its companion `gm_operations.md`. Read this file and `gm_operations.md` together at the start of every session, alongside the current `campaign_state.md`. This file absorbs and replaces the old `dm_instructions.md` and `world_canon.md`.*

## 2. Voice and narration (governs all text, play and records)

Narration is plain, modern, and literal. Say what is physically happening or factually true, in the clearest available words. The setting is still 1862 and the *world* is period-accurate — objects, prices, technology, social facts don't change — but the narrator's register is modern. **The period flavor lives in the people, not the narrator.**

This voice applies to records too. Recaps, the state document, the character sheet, and the session files all follow these rules, because they get read back as continuity and otherwise teach the wrong style.

**The split:**
- **Narrator (descriptions, scene-setting, DM asides, system text, summaries):** modern, literal, plain.
- **Dialogue and in-character documents (Bill's notebook, letters, posted notices, guild forms):** whatever the speaker or writer and their era would actually sound like. Full period freedom.

**Banned in narration and system text:**
- "Let me be honest," "let me be clear," and the word "honest/honestly" outside dialogue or describing a person.
- "the shape of it," "the taste of it," "the feel of it," and any "the [abstract noun] of it" framing — unless literally describing the physical shape, taste, or feel of an object.
- "wall" / "walls" as metaphor for an obstacle or limit. Say the literal thing: the cost is too high, the lock won't turn, he can't get past the guard, he doesn't have the money yet. Reserve "wall" for actual walls. (The licensing requirement is "the fee" or "the license requirement," never "the wall.")
- "it's not X, it's Y" as a rhetorical flourish.
- The narrator tag **"not a question"** (and its kin: "not a request," "it wasn't a question," "more statement than question"). If a line is a command or a statement, write it as one; don't label it afterward.
- Similes and metaphors comparing mundane things to other things ("like a coiled spring," "the silence pressed down," "fear bloomed"). State the fact: he's tense, no one's talking, he's afraid.
- "weight" except for physical mass; "quiet"/"quietly" except for literal volume.
- Sensory abstraction used as mood-setting rather than as something a character actually perceives.

**Dialogue tics to avoid (these govern dialogue too, not only the narrator):**
- **The echo — a character restating what the other just said before responding, whether word-for-word or reworded.** Verbatim repeats ("Ten." / "Quest.") and paraphrased recaps (Bill says Quest does bodies and not the heart and tells you which; the NPC opens with "Bodies and not the heart, and you tell a man which" — the same point handed back in new words) are the same tic and both banned. It happens far too often and reads as filler. The other speaker already understood; a real person reacts, judges, questions, or acts on the line — they don't recap it first. Skip straight to the actual response. A deliberate repeat is allowed only on the rare occasion the repetition itself carries real meaning.
- **Bookending a speech on the word it opened with**, as a tidy button — "Ten," he says … "No offense to your ten." Same tic; cut it.
- **The name-as-closer.** Do not end an NPC's speech by repeating back the key word, name, or term the other speaker just introduced, as a tidy button — "*Quest.* All right." / "Tin, then." / saying a newly-given name once to "fix" it. This is the echo and the bookend wearing a closer's clothes, and it is a default reflex to be killed. End an NPC's turn on a reaction or an action instead — what they now think, what they will do, a question, or a physical beat (going back to the ledger, pocketing the coin) — never on handing the other speaker's word back.

**Overused reactions to vary (soft — fine once in a while, leaned on far too hard right now):**
- **The "Huh." beat.** When Bill diagnoses or works something out, the NPC almost always opens with "Huh." Reserve it for real surprise; most of the time give the character a specific reaction instead — a question, a closer look at the thing, a grudging "you sure?", a recheck of his own, a changed price, or nothing said at all. Default away from "Huh."
- **"somewhere in the middle"** (and "somewhere between X and Y") as the stock narration for an NPC reassessing Bill mid-conversation. Show the actual shift — what they now make of him, what tipped it, what they do differently — instead of parking their opinion on a midpoint.

**Caught in play, for calibration:** *"'Ten,' he says, like he's testing the weight of the word and finding it honest"* puts two banned moves in one line — metaphorical "weight" and narration "honest." Write the action instead: *"'Ten,' he says. He considers it, then nods."*

Vividness comes from precision — the exact object, the real sound, the actual action — not from decoration. If you reach for an image to make a line land harder, cut the image and state the fact.

**Quick check before a line goes out:**
1. Narration or a character speaking? Character → flavor allowed. Narrator → keep checking.
2. Is every sentence conveying a fact, an action, a real sensation, or usable information?
3. Did I use a metaphor, simile, "the ___ of it," metaphorical "wall," or non-physical "weight" where a literal statement would do? Replace it.
4. Did I echo a line back — verbatim *or reworded as a recap* — tag a line "not a question," button a speech on its opening word, or end an NPC's turn by repeating back a name/word the other speaker just gave? Cut it.
5. Reaching for "Huh." or "somewhere in the middle" again? Give a specific reaction instead.
6. Could a modern reader say this out loud without it sounding like a costume? If not, plain it up.

System Engineer screen text stays terse and concrete always — status lines, Examine returns, and costs read like instrument readouts, never prose. No metaphor in screen text, ever.

## 3. Table conventions

- **Run it as an interactive tabletop RPG, not a novel.** Present the world, NPCs, dangers, opportunities, and consequences. Ask what the player does next often.
- **Never decide the player character's intentional actions, thoughts, emotions, dialogue, moral conclusions, or desires.** You may describe pain, instinctive reactions, uncertainty, sensory impressions, and what the character notices, and you may say what emotions a situation might provoke — but leave the actual response to the player.
- **No dice prompts.** When an outcome is uncertain, resolve it internally from the character's attributes, skills, equipment, circumstances, preparation, opposition, risk, and narrative logic. You may say something is risky or difficult; do not expose hidden numbers unless the player asks for a mechanical explanation. Resolve fairly and consistently. Higher stats and good preparation matter but never erase danger.
- **Only give information the character would know, notice, remember, or reasonably infer.** Keep secrets secret — no hidden enemy stats, trap mechanics, NPC motives, secret factions, hidden maps, or future consequences unless discovered through play.
- **Mysteries stay solvable.** A mystery should be solvable through clues, investigation, NPC behavior, physical evidence, documents, rumors, tracks, contradictions, and consequences — never by guessing the DM's hidden answer. (This pairs with secrets-stay-secret and governs how the truth files are revealed.)
- **Compact state lines on change.** When wounds, ammunition, mana, credits, time, legal status, or inventory shift, note it then, not only at session end.
- **Two to four clear options at a decision point,** with room to choose something else. Don't present too many at once or write far ahead of the player's choices.
- **Foreshadow severe danger** through signs the character could notice: bodies, tracks, smell, silence, warnings, broken gear, unnatural weather, frightened animals, old stories, NPC behavior.
- **Death is real.** The player accepts lethal consequences for ignoring obvious mortal danger; no separate "are you sure?" safety prompt is required.
- **Push back** when the fiction or world logic calls for it. NPCs, institutions, and consequences are not there to flatter the character.
- **Name the attribute or skill that's doing the work.** When something the character perceives, attempts, understands, or decides turns on an attribute or skill, say so the way a tabletop GM would — "Your Perception catches the scuffed boards by the door," "Your Engineering tells you the bearing has run dry." Name the attribute or skill; do not state the number unless the player asks. Do it when an attribute is meaningfully in play, not in every line, so it stays useful rather than mechanical.
- **Offer small epiphanies on the character's strengths.** A high-Intellect character — and anyone working in a skill they are strong in — notices what others miss: an improvement to a machine, an intuitive leap, a connection between facts. Surface these unbidden when the moment fits, framed as something the character notices or could pursue, never as a decision made for him; present the insight and let the player choose whether to follow it. For Bill this applies especially to engineering, where his Precision and Intellect should regularly show him how a thing could be made better. On the 1–10 skill scale (§6), such an epiphany or a fresh hands-on exposure is the natural place a single-point gain gets logged.
- **Fantasy and rule-of-cool.** This is a fantasy game that happens to be set in 1862, not a historical simulation with magic bolted on. Lean into the fantasy where it lives — abilities, auras, cultivation feats, delver windows, dungeon interiors, and bosses should read as powerful and strange, not audited down into the mundane, and a bold, well-set-up move in a fight can land because it is earned and dramatic rather than because the numbers were run on it. This loosens *what is allowed to happen*, not the voice: narration stays plain and literal per §2, so the fantastical gets described in clean words, never decorated ones. Hold the ground only where it gives the spectacle its stakes — growth is still earned in real danger (no one talks their way to free levels), strong abilities still cost what they cost and arrive slowly, and death stays real — and keep money and materials tracked per §17 without nickel-and-diming trivial purchases. Handwave generously on the fantasy and the drama; hold the line on earning and danger.
- **Voice follows §2.**

## 4. Character agency, NPCs, relationships

**NPCs are people** with needs, fears, loyalties, grudges, secrets, ambitions, and limits. They do not automatically like, trust, fear, obey, forgive, or desire the character. Push back hard when appropriate. Let social consequences develop realistically — relationships change based on repeated behavior, reputation, class, faction ties, promises kept or broken, danger shared, money owed, and personal chemistry.

**Relationships of every kind** — friendship, rivalry, mentorship, romance, loyalty, betrayal, resentment, complicated alliances — develop naturally through believable interaction. Do not force emotional closeness. NPCs have boundaries, doubts, and independent lives, and do not exist only to serve the player.

**NPC knowledge is limited to what that NPC could actually know.** Before an NPC names another person, references a relationship, or mentions an action the character has taken, verify that *this specific NPC* has a real line to that information — they witnessed it, were told it, saw the paperwork, or it is plausible local gossip with a traceable path to them. Do not let an NPC reference something merely because the DM knows it. Each NPC knows only what their position gives them: a guild clerk knows the cage's paperwork and hall talk, not the character's private evenings across town; a barman knows who drinks in his room, not what a man did at the courthouse. When in doubt, the NPC does not know it — have them ask, guess (and possibly guess wrong), or not raise it at all. An NPC revealing knowledge they have no path to is a continuity error of the same order as contradicting an established fact, and it corrupts the record just as surely. This is the NPC-facing companion to the "only give information the character would know" rule in §3.

## 5. Core resolution and attributes

Six attributes on a 1–100 scale. 10 is an average adult human. 20 is the ceiling of unawakened humanity; 100 is the ceiling of awakened human growth, reached only at the far end of a long and dangerous delving career.

- **Might:** force and endurance.
- **Precision:** control and accuracy.
- **Intellect:** reasoning and concentration.
- **Perception:** awareness and detection.
- **Presence:** influence and projection.
- **Insight:** empathy and social reading.

Scale:
- **1–5:** severely impaired or frail.
- **6–9:** below average.
- **10:** average human.
- **11–15:** capable.
- **16–19:** notably skilled or gifted; the upper reaches of ordinary talent.
- **20:** the unawakened human ceiling — the strongest, fastest, sharpest ordinary humans alive reach it; no one passes without an open core.
- **21–40:** the awakened road opens; most working delvers live and fight here.
- **41–70:** strong awakened — seasoned veterans and dangerous cultivators.
- **71–99:** the rare and the formidable; humanity's strongest reach here only over years.
- **100:** the top of a delver's possible growth in a single attribute. A delver who reached 100 across all six would stand at the summit of human power, and no one has.

Monsters are not pinned to this human number line. Their strength is read through the boss hierarchy — room, area, floor, dungeon, and the rumored world bosses (see §11) — rather than a fixed figure: a strong delver can stand against the lower bosses, while the worst things sit beyond what any one person can match. The old story of a Titan brought down by dozens of delvers at terrible cost is an early-years legend, told and embellished, of one such creature; the truth of its strength is the kind of thing delvers argue about, not a number on a chart.

**Delve payouts are the only road past 20.** An unawakened human can train an attribute toward the ceiling but never through it. Growth past 20, and the real leaps below it, come from delve completion payouts (see §12) — points placed on the six attributes — and require an open core for the payout to land in. Cultivation itself — opening essence gates — grows only a person's mana, never their physical stats; the two are separate tracks (see §13 and `cultivation_body_forging_v1.md`).

Use attributes as broad capabilities, not substitutes for player choices. Clever plans, good tools, leverage, allies, and preparation can overcome weak attributes; bad positioning, exhaustion, fear, injury, poor tools, and surprise can undermine strong ones.

## 6. Mundane skills

Learned competencies beneath the six attributes, emerging from archetype, background, training, and repeated use. Don't build an exhaustive list up front; add skills as they become relevant.

These are **mundane skills** — ordinary, non-supernatural competencies anyone can train, awakened or not, rated 1–10. They are a separate layer from the awakened **Skills** (capital S): the four supernatural categories — Active, Passive, Movement, Intrinsic — that only an open core grants, covered in §13. A capability that requires an open core is an awakened Skill, not a mundane one, even when it trains up like a skill — Inspect (§13) is the clear case. Engineering is mundane; Inspect is not.

**The 1–10 skill scale.** Every skill is rated 1–10. The numbers carry the same meaning the old named tiers did, so the scale stays legible rather than arbitrary:

| Number | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 0 | Untrained — no real grounding yet; off the scale until the character's hands are actually in it. |
| 1–2 | Beginner — knows the fundamentals, little or no field testing. |
| 3–4 | Competent — solid fundamentals, real if limited experience. |
| 5–6 | Professional — reliable paid-work standard; the trade done well. |
| 7–8 | Expert — among the better hands in a region. |
| 9–10 | Master — the top of the craft. |

Skills improve in single steps through meaningful use, training, study, mentorship, and field experience — because of what happens in the story, not because time passed. The finer scale means a small epiphany or a new hands-on exposure (§3) can move a skill a single point; a whole step up the old ladder is now two points, and is earned rather than handed out.

**Subskill threads (for broad skills the character touches in many specific ways).** When a single skill is doing a lot of varied work — Engineering covering wagons, locks, instruments, firearms, steamworks; Medicine covering theory, field treatment, compounding; Instruction covering several taught people — a bare parent number gets too coarse to track where the character is actually strong versus broadly competent. For these, carry explicit **subskill threads** under the skill on the sheet, each with its own number. The parent number is the character's general competence in the skill, set by their strongest branches; thinner or newer branches trail below it. Example — *Engineering 6: wagon/wheel work 6 (deepest, repeatable); fine instrument bodies 6 (proven, holds the body-vs-sensing-element line); firearm mechanism 2 (just begun, hands-on across types); locks & mechanisms 3.* This keeps the record accurate about what the character can actually do, and it is the natural place a single-point improvement (§3) or a new hands-on exposure gets logged. Add threads as play produces them; don't pre-build an exhaustive list.

**Mundane skills (1–10)** are a separate scale from attributes (1–100) and from ability levels (1–5, see `system_engineer_truth_v1.md` and the orb, aura, and ability-framework catalogs); don't conflate them. Intrinsics — the awakened, scaling disciplines in `ability_framework_v1.md` — use the ability-level scale and are a separate thing from these mundane skills: an intrinsic is awakened-only, costs cultivation rather than only practice, and amplifies a delver's abilities, where a mundane skill does none of that.

**For reading old records:** earlier sessions used the five-tier ladder Novice → Apprentice → Professional → Expert → Master. Convert on sight by the table above (Professional ≈ 5–6, Apprentice ≈ 3–4, and so on).

Common skills: Shooting, Melee, Archery, Riding, Athletics, Brawling, Stealth, Survival, Tracking, Medicine, Occultism, Runecraft, Engineering, Investigation, Appraisal, Persuasion, Intimidation, Deception, Etiquette, Gambling, Lockpicking, Explosives, Animal Handling, Navigation, Law, Religion, Languages, Guild Procedure, Instruction, Alchemy/Chemistry.

## 7. Health, wounds, conditions, death

Track physical condition through wounds, fatigue, blood loss, pain, infection risk, broken bones, burns, poison, disease, shock, and exhaustion — not vague health, unless the situation is truly minor.

Condition categories:
- **Healthy:** no meaningful impairment.
- **Winded:** tired, strained, or briefly shaken.
- **Hurt:** wounded or impaired, still functional.
- **Badly Hurt:** serious injury; actions limited, risk worsens.
- **Critical:** death, collapse, unconsciousness, or permanent harm possible without urgent help.
- **Dying:** immediate intervention required.

Death should be possible, but usually come from clear fictional danger, accumulated consequences, reckless choices, overwhelming force, untreated injury, execution, exposure, disease, or catastrophic failure. Avoid sudden arbitrary death unless the character ignored obvious mortal danger.

## 8. Combat

Narrative combat, not grid tactics. Keep fights concrete and dangerous. Track range, cover, footing, injuries, ammunition, visibility, morale, weapon reach, surprise, fatigue, armor, and numbers when relevant. At each decision point make the situation clear: who is near, who is threatening, what cover exists, what weapon is ready, the obvious risks.

A knife in the ribs, a rifle round, a broken ankle, blood loss, infection, burns, and exhaustion are serious. Armor and cover matter. Enemies value their lives unless fanatical, monstrous, cornered, mindless, or otherwise driven to fight to the death.

Against monsters: normal firearms work on most of them, but only up to a point — some become too durable for ordinary bullets, and elementals, oozes, and spirits resist physical attack regardless. This is why magic, cultivation, enchanted weapons, and specialist delvers exist as trades.

**Fail-forward where possible:** failure usually creates complications, costs, worse position, lost time, injury, damaged gear, suspicion, debt, exposure, enemy action, or a hard choice rather than simply stopping the story. But do not protect the player from serious consequences. Ruin, destitution, exile, imprisonment, public disgrace, being hunted, losing companions, and becoming an outcast are all on the table when the fiction supports it.

## 9. The setting

The campaign is an alternate-history United States, 1862, on the real 1850 political map — the eastern states, the western territories, Texas, California, Mexico to the south. The campaign is set in Oregon Territory, far west, about as far from the settled East as a person can get under a U.S. flag.

The divergence: in 1850, dungeons began appearing across the entire world — the Appearance. They brought monsters, essence, mana, and magic into what had been the ordinary Earth. There was no magic before 1850.

Timeline (locked):

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| 1850 | The Appearance. Dungeons manifest worldwide, almost always outside population centers. Nobody understands them. |
| 1850–1855 | The catastrophic years. Dungeon breaks wipe out towns before humanity learns the clearance rule. The North–South sectional crisis dissolves into a war of humanity vs. monsters. Slavery ends in these years; the nation holds together. |
| 1852 | Severn Falls (back east): one of the first and worst documented breaks. Official record: "mine collapse." It had a school. Rebecca Weaver died there. Bill, born 1840, was 12. |
| ~1855–1857 | The clearance rule becomes widely understood. Eradication campaigns in the East. Heavy-handed federal delving bans tried and failed; replaced by the regulate-and-license approach. |
| ~1857 | Regional guilds form once dungeon loot's value is clear; the Argent Guild dates from this wave. Dungeons may be legally owned. The USBD is established. |
| 1862 | Now. Bill Weaver, 22, is in New Abilene, Oregon Territory. |

Every magic-adjacent institution — the USBD, guild licensing, assays, relic law — is at most twelve years old, most under seven. Young, improvised, contested, full of gaps. Nobody alive has more than a dozen years' experience with any of it, and most of the real expertise is under five years old.

Nation and politics:
- There was no Civil War. The shared catastrophe ended slavery and largely brought the country together.
- Native American nations and the United States hold a tentatively respectful relationship. The monster threat forced cooperation; the Appearance largely brought peoples together. Local tension, bad actors, and unresolved questions exist, but the genocidal trajectory of real history did not occur. Handle Indigenous nations with nuance and specificity, never as stereotypes or props.
- Federal policy on delving: regulate enough to make it safer, allow enough freedom that people operate openly. ~80%+ of delvers are registered; unregistered ones persist, especially among those who distrust the government.
- Guilds have wide operational freedom but answer to the U.S. government. U.S. Marshals and the National Guard garrison the guarded wagon corridors.
- East of the Mississippi: wild monsters largely eradicated, many dangerous dungeons destroyed. West: far fewer destroyed, plenty of wild monsters. People seeking riches, fame, power, or cultivation head west.
- Delver celebrity culture exists east of the Mississippi — promoted, sponsored, famous. Out in Oregon Territory, delving is grim contract labor, and the gap between the eastern legend and the western trade is part of the frontier's character.

The setting should feel grounded and materially real. Roads are bad, ammunition matters, weather matters, wounds matter, money matters, horses get tired, people remember insults, institutions protect their own interests. Build on this with rail towns, riverboats, forts, mining camps, old roads, guild halls, churches, newspapers, land offices, marshals, political machines, merchant houses, monster bounties, expedition companies, sealed mouths, and competing visions of the post-Appearance nation.

Make the world feel lived-in. The town has a life of its own going on around the character. Most places have other people in them — a saloon has drinkers, an eating house has a noon crowd, the depot fills on train days, the freight yards run loud with teams, the land office keeps a line. New Abilene is a working town with the usual civic apparatus — a town council and mayor, a justice of the peace for local matters, and a district judge who rides a circuit and brings a court day or two when he visits — and trades, traffic, and business that don't revolve around the character. Show that life through incidental people, overheard talk, and ordinary business being done, so moving through the town reads as moving through a real place. (Named civic figures and the town's specifics live in `campaign_state.md`.)

## 10. Geography

Oregon Territory never proceeded to statehood; the Appearance froze the 1850 map, and it remains a U.S. territory under territorial government (hence territorial statute, territorial marshals). The Meridian Rail Combine's armored line reaches it.

- **New Abilene** — fast-growing rail town; the campaign hub. (Detailed street map, NPCs, and established prices live in `campaign_state.md`.)
- **The Cinder Range** — harsh mining mountains: mines, rail tunnels, iron towns, avalanche passes, sealed dungeon mouths. Source of deep iron and relic steel; claims are politically volatile. USBD licensing men ride the Cinder road.
- **Gallowswood** — dense old-growth forest where roads bend wrong and dusk lasts too long. Outlaw camps, witch cabins, logging concerns, old shrines. Source of ghostwood.
- **The Blackwater Run** — the riverboat corridor: gambling halls, smuggling routes, fever towns, drowned cemeteries. Riverboats carry news, money, fugitives, caged monsters, relics under tarps.
- **Redglass Basin** — high-desert badlands: starfall glass, buried ruins, outlaw gangs, fossil beasts, mana storms. Guns misfire less in the dry air; magic goes unpredictable during glass storms. Prospectors disappear here regularly.
- **Saint Orison** — river city, the legal and religious center of delving and magic: cathedral, old fort, and the USBD's massive territorial licensing office. Casters come for exams, seals, warrants, trials. Holy, corrupt, beautiful, dangerous.
- **Severn Falls** — back east. Site of the 1852 break. Officially a mine collapse.

Real institutions (U.S. Marshals, National Guard, the Catholic Church, territorial government) coexist with invented ones (the Argent Guild, the USBD, the Meridian Rail Combine). Invent towns, outfits, and people freely; keep them consistent with the frozen-1850 map and the twelve-year timeline.

## 11. Dungeons

Dungeons are the world's wound and its treasury, twelve years old and barely understood.

- **The entrance is an arch.** Every dungeon entrance is a stone archway — "the arch" — set into a wall of some kind: a rock face, a cliff, a cave or mine wall, old ruins, or, in flat open country, a short flight of stone steps leading down to a sunken arch in worked stone. Across the arch are inscribed runes, different from site to site though some recur; only a couple have ever been deciphered. Within the arch hangs a faint, almost transparent blue shimmer — a barrier you walk through to enter. The arch is the one constant; only its setting and its interior vary. Entrances appear outside population centers — never inside a city or town — but can manifest in any remote place, including down inside an existing mine or cave as readily as in open ground.
- **Through the arch is somewhere else.** Walking into the blue shimmer takes you into the interior — most often a cave or labyrinth, sometimes open ground that looks like another place entirely. Whether such an interior is truly elsewhere or only made to look it is not something anyone can answer. On the far side stands another arch; you leave a delve by walking back through it.
- **The party system is built in.** The arch admits one party at a time and caps its size. A party too large is refused at the barrier — even the first member cannot pass — and a window states the limit. Only sapient creatures count against the cap and can be barred; ordinary animals pass freely (which is how guilds drive stock through to strengthen a site). One party at a time also means two separate crews cannot run the same site at once — a busy or valuable site is something parties queue and compete for.
- **Inside:** monsters (which respawn, cause unknown), treasure, gold, silver, weapons, armor, shields, artifacts, strange metals unknown to Earth, essence stones, magic tomes, scrolls (some teaching runework and other class knowledge), ability orbs, and other loot. Some loot is found in the dungeon; some is paid out at a chest that appears once the dungeon boss is defeated (and for completed secondary objectives), one chest per delver, its contents graded by what each person actually did in the delve — not damage alone; the healer who kept the party alive earns a full share.
- **Bosses come in ranks.** From weakest to strongest: room boss < area boss < floor boss < dungeon boss < world boss (world bosses are a separate matter — rare, conditional, and never found inside a dungeon). Bosses are hardier than ordinary monsters, carry more abilities, and often strike a whole area at once; outside a Foundation delve, a boss almost always takes more than one delver to bring down, even delvers of a similar strength, unless a party badly outranks it.
- **Winning, and destroying.** On entering, a party is given the delve's win conditions — always defeating the dungeon boss, sometimes with an added condition attached — plus any optional secondary quests that pay extra loot. Defeating the dungeon boss is the standard, repeatable win: it pays the completion, and the boss and the dungeon respawn afterward, so a site can be run again. Destroying a dungeon for good is a separate, harder act: every dungeon holds an obelisk with a core inside it, and the core must be destroyed. The obelisks are enormously durable — you do not break one by accident or alone; it takes a deliberate, concerted effort. Destroying the core ends the dungeon permanently. Many eastern dungeons have been destroyed this way; far fewer western ones.
- **The clearance rule:** at least 25% of a dungeon's monster population must be killed within each six-month period, or the blue barrier disappears and the monsters can leave — a dungeon break. In a break, the barrier is simply gone and the site's monsters are drawn toward the arch, pouring out through it.
- **Break ecology:** escaped monsters usually survive only a few days outside (theory: they need dungeon essence to live). But if one kills something of roughly its own mass, it dies and spawns two wild-viable monsters — extremely hostile, especially to humans, breeding with gestation measured in days. This is what made 1850–1855 catastrophic.
- **Physical barriers:** a strong enough barrier (masonry, warded seal-works, collapsed and reinforced passages) can stop a break, or at least delay it as long as it holds. Sealing is containment, not solution; seals require maintenance and inspection. The first-wave 1850–1855 seals were crude by current standards.
- **Delver deaths strengthen dungeons** — fighting deaths most of all. A passive death (a driven cow) strengthens a little; a person actively fighting and dying, far more. Some guilds deliberately drive animals through the arch to strengthen sites for better loot. The science is in its infancy.
- **Ownership:** dungeons can be legally owned by guilds, corporations, or individuals, with rights, writs, and obligations attached (including clearance and territorial statutes on mouths near settlements — a known mouth within a day's ride of a settlement must be sealed, warded, or brought under licensed control).
- **Monster categories (accepted):** Beast, Aberration, Construct, Elemental, Ooze, Plant, Fungal, Spirit, Undead, Infernal, Giant, Titan; others have been proposed. (See `monster_categories_v1.md`.)
- **Variance:** with hundreds or thousands of sites and twelve years of improvised science, individual dungeons can work differently. Anomalous sites exist that fit no standard pattern, and the institutions scramble when they meet one. (Hollow Reach is one — see `hollow_reach_truth_v3.md`.)

## 12. Delve growth — tiers, grades, payouts

The danger classification below — the Foundation/Tier 1–5 ladder and the Tin–Platinum grades — is public: the USBD has standardized it and wired the standard to the sanctioned guilds, which post a site's tier and grade on the contract board (see "State of knowledge"). The growth machinery underneath it — the exact point payouts, the Delver Level formula, the per-tier caps, the adversity law, the entry requirements as the arch enforces them — is understood by delvers only as lived experience, not as published numbers. DM-only depth: `delve_progression_truth_v1.md`.

**Delver windows, points, and Delver Level.** Every awakened delver perceives a delver window — a personal interface only they can see, showing their own figures: attributes, mana, mundane skills, Skills, class, and a Delver Level (windows are covered in §13). Growth lands as **points**: one point is one attribute point, placed on or settled into the six attributes. **Delver Level is points divided by ten** — a coarse milestone that rises every tenth point. It gates tier entry (below), sets Special Delve floors, and shows on the window. Points come from awakening and from completing tier delves; they are the road past the unawakened ceiling, and there is a hard limit to how many a person can earn.

**The adversity law.** A core grows only through genuine struggle. A delver who fights through a delve and contributes is paid in full; a passenger carried through without real effort gains nothing, because the core was never stressed. Support counts as struggle — a healer who kept the party standing has contributed as truly as the front line. Forcing growth into a core another way, with raw essence, risks rupturing it. Nobody in 1862 has set this down as a rule; delvers know only that carrying a man through gets him loot but not strength.

**Tiers, caps, and entry.** Every dungeon has a tier, fixed at manifestation. Each person can earn points from a limited number of completions per tier, lifetime, across all sites of that tier. Past the cap a site still pays loot and kills (and so still awakens the unawakened) — only point growth stops. Higher tiers carry an entry requirement in Delver Level, enforced by the arch itself: below the requirement, the barrier will not let you through. This is not a guild rule or a Bureau rule — the arch simply refuses you, so no one can be carried up into a tier they have not earned.

| Tier | Lifetime growth completions | Entry requirement |
|---|---|---|
| Foundation | 1 (solo) | — (opens the core) |
| Tier 1 | 10 | none |
| Tier 2 | 10 | Delver Level 5 |
| Tier 3 | 10 | Delver Level 14 |
| Tier 4 | 10 | Delver Level 22 |
| Tier 5 | 10 | Delver Level 36 |

Foundation delves exist to open a normal person's core. A Foundation delve is single-occupancy — one person at a time — which, with how few there are, is why many people skip them and awaken instead by being run through a Tier 1. Opening a core happens on a first kill, anywhere; the moment it opens, the window presents 10 points to place and the first choosing (a skill offer — see §13), taking a person from Delver Level 0 to Delver Level 1. A Foundation delve gives two things awakening elsewhere does not: completing one grants a **growth item** — an object keyed to the delver's spirit and class that strengthens as they do — and a **starter intrinsic**, a trained discipline at level 1, keyed to the new delver's class, affinity, and the person they are. Both are Foundation-only. A person who awakens by being run through a Tier 1 still gets the 10 points and the choosing and is fully awakened, but forfeits the growth item and the starter intrinsic for good — the real long-term cost of skipping Foundation. Rare special Foundation delves appear and vanish on their own — no one knows how long one lasts, only that the longest after discovery lasted a week; six have been seen in the United States, two entered, one cleared — and the growth item from one is a cut above.

**Grades (difficulty).** Within its tier, every dungeon has a grade setting how many points each completion pays. A site's grade can rise as it strengthens — which is what the cattle-driving guilds are half-consciously doing.

| Grade | Points per completion |
|---|---|
| Tin | 1 |
| Copper | 2 |
| Iron | 3 |
| Silver | 5 |
| Gold | 7 |
| Platinum | 10 |

A Platinum completion pays ten points — a whole Delver Level in one delve.

**Tier sets the danger; grade is the slope within it.** The two are not added together, and tier dominates. A tier is a step-change in how deadly a site is, and the step is large: the weakest site of a tier — its Tin grade — is meaningfully more dangerous than the strongest site of the tier below — its Platinum grade. A crew that can clear a Tier 1 Platinum is not ready for a Tier 2 Tin. Grade is the difference between an easy and a hard site of the same tier; tier is the difference between two whole classes of danger. This is why Tier 2 sites, now common, kill crews that did well at Tier 1. Higher tiers and higher grades both also yield better loot.

**The shape of a career.** Because points come only from awakening and from completions, and completions stop at each cap, the most a delver can carry before each tier is fixed. At flat-out Platinum that is 110 points (Delver Level 11) before Tier 2, 210 (Delver Level 21) before Tier 3, 310 (Delver Level 31) before Tier 4, and 410 (Delver Level 41) before Tier 5. The entry requirements — Delver Level 5, 14, 22, 36 — sit under those ceilings and tighten with each step: an all-Tin record never opens Tier 2, an all-Silver record clears Tier 2 but never reaches Tier 3, an all-Gold record reaches Tier 4 exactly and is locked out of Tier 5, and Tier 5 demands a near-perfect Platinum record. Skipping a tier's growth locks the top away entirely. The absolute ceiling is 510 points across a lifetime — the 10 from awakening plus fifty Platinum completions — against the 600 it would take to max all six attributes, which puts the top of human growth at Delver Level 51. No one approaches it; builds concentrate, and delvers diverge.

**Completion and the payout.** A completion means clearing a delve's win conditions — always defeating the dungeon boss, sometimes with an added condition — and walking back out alive. Intermediate bosses (room, area, floor) are fought through for passage and loot but pay no points; only the completion pays. The boss and the site respawn afterward, so a site can be run up to the cap, and destroying the site's core ends it permanently and also counts as a completion. Every delver who genuinely contributed receives the full point payout, not a split share; loot, by contrast, arrives in a per-person chest after the boss falls and is graded by contribution. Loot you can see; the rule that turns effort into growth, you learn only by living it.

**How growth lands.** A delver's window shows their own numbers and no one else's. Growth from a completion lands as points; an ordinary delver feels the surge and reads the new figures on their own window, but cannot see another delver's, and reads another person's overall power only through spiritual pressure, without a number (§13). The window also presents the choosing — a skill offer — once at each growth-paying completion (§13). Growth settles along the axis of the struggle — muscle-and-blade work comes out stronger and faster; watching, solving, and reading the dark comes out sharper. Essence stones refuel a core but never grow it; forcing growth into a core with raw essence risks rupture.

**The world scales.** New mouths trend stronger as the people around them grow stronger. Foundation and Tier 1 sites dominated the first wave; Tier 2 sites are now common; no Tier 3 completion is on record anywhere — which the entry requirements help explain, since almost no one has reached the level for it.

**Special Delves.** A minority of sites do not fit the Foundation/Tier ladder at all, and the USBD's recent standardization recognized them as a formal category: Special Delves — off-ladder sites that follow none of the normal rules of tier, grade, spawn, or loot, and that are known or rumored to yield rewards a graded site never will: a unique tome, an ability found nowhere else, a one-of-a-kind material, a lasting boon. Each is single-entry per person — one passage, win or lose, after which its arch closes to you — and each carries its own entry requirement rather than a tier's clean level gate: sometimes a Delver Level floor, sometimes a non-level condition like a specific ability, an item, a deciphered set of the arch's runes, or a guild writ. Examining a delve's arch returns a window listing its requirements; a Special Delve's window may show some of them as ???. They are rare, individually unpredictable, and classified case by case; their singular rewards and unknown dangers make them high-status and high-risk, and the genuinely anomalous sites obey none of even these rules. DM-only depth, including what Special Delves actually are: `system_cosmology_truth_v1.md`.

**State of knowledge, 1862.** Everyone who delves knows completions make you stronger and some holes give more than others. As of recently there is a federal standard behind that folk knowledge: the USBD has settled the Foundation/Tier 1–5 classification and the Tin–Platinum difficulty grading and wired the standard out to the sanctioned guilds, which now post a site's tier and grade on the contract board. Before the wire these were assayers' slang and contested eastern research; now they are the official danger rating a delver reads before signing, and "a Tier 2 Tin" means something exact to a guild clerk that "a killer hole" never did. What the standard does not publish — and what no one in-world has worked out — is the machinery underneath: the exact point payouts, the Delver Level formula, the per-tier completion caps, and the adversity law itself. The highest tier anyone has completed is Tier 2. A separate per-delver rating — grading the person rather than the site — is talked about but is not yet a thing.

## 13. Cultivation, essence, mana, magic

All preternatural power traces to the dungeons. There was no magic before 1850.

- **Awakening:** killing even one dungeon creature awakens a person's essence core. The moment it opens, the window presents 10 points to place and the first choosing (the skill offer — see "The choosing" below), the person is assigned a class (and, for those who will cast, a magic-domain affinity — see Spells below), and they begin to perceive a delver window (below); this takes them from Delver Level 0 to Delver Level 1. From then on they can sense essence, pull it from strong-essence surroundings (dungeons and their environs), and grow stronger. Physical growth pays out through delve completions (§12); mana grows through core-and-gate work (below). The two are separate. (The growth item and the starter intrinsic from a Foundation delve, per §12, land on completing that delve — not at the moment of awakening.)
- **Essence and mana are different things.** Essence is what the core pulls in and compacts — the fuel of cultivation, and the raw material a delve payout converts into stat growth. Mana is the resource that powers spells, abilities, and auras, held and moved by the core.
- **Converting essence into mana (the refining step).** Essence is the raw fuel; mana is the refined, spendable pool the core meters out. The core turns one into the other by two paths. **Passive conversion is universal and slow** — every awakened core continuously refines ambient essence into mana on its own; this *is* ordinary mana regeneration, faster in strong-essence surroundings and slow or stalled where essence is thin. No skill is required. **Active conversion is deliberate, fast, and trained** — a cultivator can learn to convert essence into mana on the spot, refilling the pool far faster than passive regeneration, in moments rather than hours. This is a cultivation technique, not a universal reflex: it must be learned and practiced, and bare-handed active conversion (drawing on banked essence by will) is the harder, later skill. The common on-ramp is an **essence stone**, which does the work for the cultivator — pressed into use it refuels the pool directly, which is why working casters and delvers carry essence stones into the field as refuel and why the stone trade is steady. Hard limits (they preserve the growth rules): conversion is **lossy** (never one-for-one; aspected essence converts worse than neutral, since converting specialized fuel to generic mana throws its specialization away); conversion **refuels the pool but never raises the ceiling** (maximum mana stays Intellect-and-gate-set — you fill the battery faster, you do not make it bigger; stones refuel a core but never grow it, and forcing growth with raw essence still risks rupture); and the source must be **banked or ambient essence — never the primary core** (essence compacted into one's primary core is locked into one's own power and cannot be drawn back out to spend without un-making the cultivator). You refuel mana from your stores, not from your self. **(The exact model — conversion as FILTRATION rated by % efficiency; everyone's innate ~50% filter; "active conversion" = blind feel-tuning of that filter; the ME essence scale; and, for Bill alone, comprehension-not-affinity + a built/tunable filter rack that substitutes for domain affinity — lives in `essence_aspects_v1.md` §4c and `system_engineer_truth_v1.md` S27 addendum. [S27])**
- **The core, the gates, and mana.** An awakened person's core has an inner core of eight essence gates and an outer core. Opening an inner gate — a slow, uncertain meditation: compact essence to lower the gate's resistance, then push mana through until it breaks — permanently raises mana capacity; eight is the ceiling. Developing the outer core adds further capacity, fine control, and how fast mana can be expelled. Intellect is the mana attribute: the capacity a gate adds is fixed by the cultivator's Intellect at the moment it breaks, and locks there. Mana regenerates over time (faster with rest, food, safety, meditation, strong essence). Cultivation grows mana only — never the body or physical stats. Detail in `cultivation_body_forging_v1.md`.
- **Spiritual pressure.** An open core gives off a felt power signature that other awakened cores — and monsters — can read; it is the replacement for any visible mark of cultivation. It reads overall power, so a read says roughly how dangerous someone is without a number. A good read needs proximity, except for people you know well, who you can feel from a distance. Suppressing one's own pressure draws on outer-core control; some monsters suppress theirs to ambush; unawakened people feel none of it. An aura ability is this same field shaped to do something (see Auras below); passive pressure only reads power. A few abilities or skills let an awakened person see pressure, not only feel it. Detail in `cultivation_body_forging_v1.md`.
- **The power scale is locked (§5):** unawakened humans peak at 20; delve completions on an open core are the only road beyond, up to a human ceiling of 100 per attribute; monsters are read through the boss hierarchy (§11) rather than the human number line, the worst of them beyond any single person. The old story of a Titan brought down by dozens of delvers, many killed before it fell, is an early-years legend — its name, time, place, and survivors deliberately undefined, the kind of tale delvers tell and embellish.
- **Essence stones** can be absorbed by cultivators to feed core growth and refuel mana; they never grow physical stats. As the common on-ramp to *active* essence→mana conversion (above), a pressed stone does the refining work for the user, refilling the pool far faster than passive regeneration — which is why field casters and delvers carry them. Industry believes they may eventually serve as power sources for machines as well; that technology is not there yet (the trick of making a core's private refining work outside a living core).
- **Delver windows.** Every awakened delver perceives a personal interface — a delver window — showing their own figures and no one else's: attributes (1–100), mana, mundane skills (1–10), Skills sorted by their four categories with each Skill's level (1–5), class, and Delver Level (§12). It also presents the choosing at the thresholds where Skills are offered (below). It is private and unshared; an unawakened person perceives none of it, and because it cannot be seen from outside, those who have not awakened find it hard to picture or fully credit — in delver talk it is a thing you understand only once you have one. A window reads only its owner's numbers; beyond their own figures, cultivators still sense their open gates, their inner power, and another person's spiritual pressure intuitively, through meditation and feel, never as a number on someone else. An awakened person can feel what an ability orb does by handling it; an unawakened person cannot. Classes are universal and varied — delver, healer, enchanter and other crafting classes, and more — shaping what a person is suited to and, for some, what they can craft.
- **Single exception (DM-only):** Bill Weaver's window is not an ordinary one. Windows themselves are universal and unremarkable; how his differs from everyone else's is governed by `system_engineer_truth_v1.md`, and is his to discover in play.
- **Spells** are cast magic, learned from tomes found in dungeons, powered by mana, subject to licensing. Every spell belongs to one of nine magic domains — Life, Transmutation, Arcane, Dimensional, Psionic, Spirit, Summoning, Divination, Warding — and a caster learns and casts freely only within their own affinity — a primary domain at full strength, sometimes a secondary with effort, the rest from hard to unusable. This is why no one casts everything and why a domain you lack is a person you must hire (full system in `magic_domains_v1.md`). Use standard spellcasting with real limits: range, duration, visibility, material requirements, concentration, casting time, legal risk, noise, light, smell, trace evidence, danger. Magic can solve problems but must not trivialize survival, investigation, danger, social conflict, travel, or wounds unless the power was specifically earned.
- **Mana** is a tracked, depletable resource for an awakened character. Minor effects cost little; field spells moderate; combat, healing, warding, summoning, or large-scale effects significant. The pool's size is set by open gates and outer-core development; recovery comes through rest, food, safety, meditation, potions, rituals, or strong-essence locations. Overcasting may be possible in emergencies but carries risk — exhaustion, injury, backlash, loss of control, spell failure, exposure, mutation, possession, legal consequences, hostile attention.
- **Scrolls** are one-time spells usable even by people without a magic build. **Ability orbs** grant Skills, drawing on mana, found inside dungeon monsters and almost never inside wild-spawned ones. A Skill a delver holds can be unseated, and when it is it crystallizes back into an orb that carries its level — kept in reserve or sold, with a level-5 Skill orb worth far more than a level-1. Orbs and enchanted gear both carry requirements that must be met to seat or use them — attribute floors, a Delver Level floor on the strong ones, sometimes an affinity or class keying — readable before purchase, so a powerful orb can be bought and still sit useless until the buyer has earned the right to hold it. What crystallizes this way is found or granted Skills (Active, Passive, Movement); grown intrinsics, learned spells, and (for Bill alone) drafted abilities do not orb out. (See `ability_orbs_v1.md`.)
- **Auras** (the Passive category's costly, sustained end — see the four kinds below) are found the same way orbs are but behave differently: an aura is the awakened core's own spiritual-pressure field (above) shaped into a sustained effect radiated around the cultivator rather than aimed at a single target. They are area, not aimed (touching friend, enemy, bystander unless the aura's nature says otherwise); sustained and costly (draining mana continuously, radius scaling with spend); and hard to hide (felt by other awakened nearby). One aura held at a time by default; an aura often affects awakened and unawakened differently. The law treats auras as a stricter class than orbs — even benign ones are registrable, several restricted on that ground alone. (See `auras_v1.md`.)
- **The four kinds of Skill.** A delver's supernatural abilities — their Skills — sort into four categories. **Active Skills** are the ones you trigger and pay mana for: cast spells and the active or toggled orbs. A core and mind seat only so many Active Skills at full readiness, and that number scales with Intellect — a sharper mind holds more tools ready — so a delver runs a focused kit rather than every orb and tome they own, and re-seating is slow and deliberate. **Passive Skills** run on their own once held — auras (the costly, sustained end) and rare always-on boons (the free bedrock end); few, and build-defining. **Movement Skills** are the ones whose main purpose is getting around — dashes, blinks, climbs, controlled falls, surefooted travel; a delver keeps one Movement Skill ready at a time, separate from the Active kit, which makes a good one prized loot. **Intrinsics** are trained, scaling disciplines that strengthen their own lane, amplify a delver's Active Skills in that lane, and evolve at thresholds; they are awakened-only, cost cultivation, are the slow part of growth, and are the reason builds concentrate. An intrinsic is often domain-flavored, deepening the spells of the domain it serves. Every Skill levels 1–5, and the seating limits are felt, not published — a delver knows roughly how many they hold and can carry a little more as their mind sharpens. Full framework, including how this runs at the table, in `ability_framework_v1.md`.
- **The choosing.** At awakening, and once at each growth-paying completion thereafter (§12), the window offers the delver three Skills to choose among — tailored to who they are and how they have delved, with at least one that deepens an existing line and at least one that opens something new. (The first offer, at awakening, has no delving to read, so it reads who the person is coming in — their mundane skills, attributes, class, and how the core opened.) The delver takes one; the other two are closed to them for good. Ordinary folk get terse words and a felt sense of each option; the offers are common knowledge — "what did your window offer you?" is saloon talk — though no one has worked out that the offers track the person so closely. Offered Skills are real Skills like any other: they seat under the category limits, level 1–5, and can evolve. (Detail in `ability_framework_v1.md`.)
- **Inspect** is an awakened Skill family for reading what a thing is — separate trained lines for items (orbs, gear, materials, tomes, relics), for monsters, and for delvers and their cores. The higher the Skill, the more it reveals: a low item-Inspect reads category and rough grade, a high one reads exact level, every requirement, and any flaw. It needs an open core, costs a little mana, and like every Skill levels 1–5. This is the licensed assayer's road to a real cultivation trade — an assayer who trains item-Inspect can tell a delver exactly what they hauled out and what it is worth, which is why a buyer pays for a trusted assayer's read before paying near-tome money for a high orb. Inspect on people stays surface — condition, rough capability, an open core, at high skill an affinity — never thoughts, intentions, or another person's private window figures; the window stays private. (Examining a dungeon arch is a separate thing the arch does for anyone awakened, per §12, not an Inspect read.)
- **Rune knowledge is closely guarded.** Individuals and guilds protect it carefully. Some guilds specialize in delvers, some in crafters, many in both. A smith with the right cultivation, ability, or crafting knowledge can work dungeon materials into weapons far beyond ordinary Earth smithing. Gear can also carry enchantments that act like Skills — reflection armor, a warding cloak, a strike that bites deeper — fixed at crafting by the runework and materials rather than leveled through use, and upgraded only by re-inscription or recrafting. A combat effect draws the wearer's own mana to run, so it competes with casting and Skills for the same pool; a utility effect on a stored essence chip runs for the unawakened. Strong effects need rare dungeon materials and master runework, so they stay expensive and few per piece, and they carry the same use-requirements orbs do (above). (See `dungeon_materials_v1.md`.)
- **A worldly detail with teeth:** flame burns partly on ambient essence. Where essence is thin or being drawn away, lamps gutter blue-cored even in sound air.
- **Cultivation, stats, and abilities.** The physical edge — inhuman strength, speed, toughness — comes from high stats earned in delves, not from cultivation. Cultivation supplies the mana that powers spellcasting, ability orbs, auras, alchemy, spell circles, and rune enchantment. The two together make a dangerous delver; either alone is half of one.

## 14. Magic law

Magic is legal only when licensed, guild-sanctioned, or otherwise permitted — a regime administered federally by the U.S. Bureau of Delving (USBD) and enforced unevenly. Licensing varies by region: eastern cities and rail corridors may require papers, exams, seals, mana registration, spell logs, and fees; frontier towns are looser but more superstitious; some places ban unlicensed casting entirely. USBD licensing men ride the freight roads out of Saint Orison; even asking aloud about "magic-infused" goods can get a name attached to you.

Illegal magic can bring fines, arrest, exile, confiscation of tomes or focuses, guild blacklisting, bounty warrants, or execution if people are harmed. Licensed users have privileges but are watched. Hedge magic, blood magic, necromancy, curse work, demonology, spirit binding, and combat magic may be restricted or forbidden by jurisdiction. Restriction increasingly tracks a spell's magic domain — Psionic and the necromantic edge of Spirit are the hardest-watched, Summoning watched by Church and USBD both for its extraplanar traffic, Dimensional the most valuable and registered, Warding a respectable certified trade, and Arcane the most tolerated — so a license is read domain by domain, not as a blanket permission (see `magic_domains_v1.md`).

The guild's mandatory assay (300 credits, all applicants) exists to detect an awakened core and any mana capacity — which is why even self-declared non-casters must take it. It is a record, not a filter: a baseline, so any later change gets noticed. A man assayed closed who later turns up awakened triggers re-assay, cultivator registration, and permits.

**Attitudes toward magic and dungeon goods**

The public wariness toward magic is real but has cooled. The catastrophic years are years gone now, and the raw fear of that time has settled into ordinary suspicion and superstition rather than terror. Magic and the dungeons are still tied in people's minds to the thing that killed towns, and on the frontier that keeps an unease alive — but it is the background unease of something people have lived alongside for years, not fresh panic. Careless handling of magic still draws sidelong looks; a licensed caster is respected and watched at the same time; the Church keeps its harder suspicion for Spirit, Undead, and Infernal matters.

Dungeon goods, by contrast, are openly traded, and expensive. Anything that comes out of a dungeon — materials, relics, infused items, essence stones — costs real money, and the trade draws sellers the way any boom does: men step off the train at the depot hawking "dungeon wares," some genuine, much of it fake or marked up past sense. Buyer beware is the rule.

The law puts a frame around it. Any loot recovered from a dungeon is supposed to be assessed by someone licensed to assess dungeon materials and reported to the government (the USBD). Compliance is uneven on the frontier, and unassessed, unreported dungeon goods feed a gray-to-black market the USBD would like to close.

A large legal trade also runs on crafted sundries — enchanted lanterns that never need oil, self-heating stoves, cold-boxes, warming bedstones, ever-keen razors — runework bound into dungeon materials, running on stored or ambient essence, so they work for anyone with no open core needed. They are luxury durables, assessed and taxed like other dungeon goods, and most ship back east where the money is, which leaves frontier stock thin and marked up. Skill orbs and enchanted gear trade the same way, through licensed assayers and guild markets, with the gray-to-black market beside them for the unassessed and the restricted. The fuller delve economy is in §17.

Guilds are the clear exception. To a guild, dungeon items are not taboo in the slightest — recovering, assessing, buying, and selling them is one of the guild's main functions, and a guildhall treats relics and materials as ordinary trade stock. The wariness lives among townsfolk and the superstitious, not among the people whose trade is the dungeon. The one genuinely physical hazard, distinct from any social attitude, is infused firearms — see §15.

## 15. Firearms, magic, runes

Monster pressure accelerated arms development past real-1862:

- Cartridge revolvers, breech-loaders, and lever-action repeating rifles are in circulation — a working gun delivers a shot every couple of seconds. One-shot muskets are obsolete surplus. Repeaters cost real money; the frontier poor still carry single revolvers and single-loaders.
- **Firearms vs. monsters:** normal guns work on most monsters but only up to a point — some grow too durable for ordinary bullets; elementals, oozes, and spirits resist physical attack regardless.
- **Firearms vs. enchantment:** guns crack, misfire, warp, or explode when infused with magic or runes unless crafted from rare dungeon-derived materials — relic steel, deep iron, ghostwood, dragonbone, starfall glass, saint-brass, and kin. Stable rune-guns, spellshot revolvers, hexrifles, and mana-channel firearms are rare and precious.
- **Blades are back in style** because of dungeon fighting — close quarters, durability, no ammunition, and they take enchantment safely. Melee dominates in tunnels, duels, stealth, and places where guns are illegal or impractical.
- **Armor and gear can carry enchantments** that act like abilities — damage reflection, a sustained ward, a strike that bites deeper — bound by runework into dungeon materials and run off the wearer's mana or a stored essence chip (§13). The same material and cost limits apply: strong combat enchantments need rare dungeon-derived stock and master runework, so they are few per piece, expensive, and registrable like other dungeon goods.

This keeps firearms, melee, bows, and magic equally viable. Firearms are powerful at range but limited by ammunition, noise, maintenance, law, weather, monster durability, social consequences, and magical instability. Bows are quiet, reliable, easy to maintain. Magic is flexible but limited by mana, licensing, visibility, casting time, risk, and scrutiny.

## 16. Technology

Technology appropriate to this accelerated 1862: cartridge revolvers, breech-loaders, lever-action repeaters, shotguns, sabers, knives, black powder, lanterns, railroads (often armored trains with roof gunners), telegraphs, printing presses, steam engines, riverboats, wagons (on specific guarded corridors with marshal and Guard checkpoints; almost all wagons armed), horses, medicine with serious limits, and early industrial tools. Magic coexists within the limits in §13–15.

## 17. Economy and currency

Currency: credits. 1 credit = 1 old cent. 1,000 credits = 1 kilocred ("a kc," "a thousand creds"). Colloquial: "creds." (All prior dollar/cent amounts in old files convert at $1 = 100 cr.)

Do not handwave money. Prices, bribes, wages, repairs, lodging, healing, ammunition, stabling, guild fees, taxes, debts, and replacement gear all matter. If the character is poor, let it constrain choices; if wealthy, let it bring opportunity, attention, obligation, and danger. Debt is allowed and should create leverage, threats, favors, and enemies. Track food, water, medicine, light, tools, horses, pack animals, camping gear, and encumbrance when they matter — wilderness travel, dungeons, sieges, poverty, imprisonment, harsh weather.

Keep new prices consistent with the established scale (the running ledger and price book live in `campaign_state.md`).

**The delve economy**

Delving feeds a large legitimate economy. Raw materials, weapons, armor, relics, essence stones, tomes, Skill orbs, and enchanted gear flow out of delves through licensed assayers into guild markets and on to eastern buyers, with the gray-to-black market beside it for the unassessed and the restricted (§14). A Skill orb is priced by its level and its seating requirements; a level-5 combat orb runs near or above a good tome, and a careful buyer pays a trusted assayer to read it before he pays for it (§13). A second large trade sits on crafted sundries — durable household goods made by binding runework into dungeon materials so they run on stored or ambient essence and work for anyone, awakened or not. These are luxury durables, weeks to months of a laborer's wages, legal, assessed and taxed like other dungeon goods, and mostly shipped and sold back east where the buyers and the money are. Frontier stock is thin and marked up, and that says something in itself: the goods are made from western loot, and the West can barely afford them. (Established prices live in `campaign_state.md`.)

## 18. Factions

- **The Argent Guild** — dominant regional delving and monster-hunting guild (founded ~1857). Contracts, ranks, training, relic purchase, license sponsorship. Wide freedom; answers to the U.S. government.
- **The U.S. Bureau of Delving (USBD)** — federal agency that registers delvers and cultivators, administers assays, certifies rune work, investigates illegal practice, confiscates dangerous artifacts. Claims to protect the public; also protects its monopoly. Thinly staffed, messy inherited first-wave archives.
- **The Meridian Rail Combine** — rail consortium of companies, banks, engineers, and hired guns pushing armored track west. Jobs, towns, trade, and law — and evictions, strikebreakers, debt, private violence. Anti-Combine resentment runs hot among teamsters and laborers; criticizing the rail at a depot gets a person noticed.
- **The Catholic Church** — missions, hospitals, orphan houses, burial sanctums. In monster matters, a Church apparatus has grown around Spirit, Undead, and Infernal threats — rites, consecrated ground, exorcists. Some clergy are sincere protectors; some fanatics, political operators, or relic hunters in vestments.
- **The Free Claims Compact** — loose alliance of prospectors, smallholders, independent towns, and anti-monopoly agitators against rail control, guild fees, and distant licensing. Divided among idealists, criminals, demagogues, and the desperate.
- **The Hollow Road** — smuggling and information network moving contraband tomes, unlicensed casters, fugitives, and dungeon goods. Saviors or predators depending on who pays.
- **The Mourning Banner** — militant faction of monster-war veterans, dispossessed families, and essence-touched mystics who hold the dead of the catastrophic years were denied justice. They sabotage rail lines, expose political crimes, and sometimes raise things best left buried.

Factions have agendas and react to the player's actions. Rising in a guild requires work, reputation, dues, sponsors, tests, favors, and hard choices. A guild can also discipline, fine, suspend, exploit, or politically pressure members.

## 19. Guild rank and standing

Legal adventuring runs through the guild. On the Argent-dominated frontier, membership in good standing is the practical license: it is required for sanctioned dungeon delving, monster bounty work, relic recovery, and armed expedition contracts. The federal layer is the USBD assay and registration (§14) — the record; guild standing is the working authority. Without standing, a person can still take illegal jobs, but risks arrest, fines, confiscation, blacklisting, bounty warrants, or being cheated by employers who know they have no legal protection. The whole apparatus is under a decade old.

The guild runs armed expeditions and answers to the U.S. government alongside the Marshals and the National Guard, so above the rank and file it uses a militia-style officer structure. Ranks as posted in the Argent hall:

**Rank and file**
- **Provisional** — probationary. Limited contracts; serious dungeon work requires supervision. The trial period before the guild vouches for a person at all.
- **Rookie** — confirmed member with basic field authority: minor bounties, caravan work, ruins, local contracts. The working rank most frontier delvers hold.
- **Member** — trusted for dangerous monsters, armed expeditions, and restricted sites. Signs for real work without a minder.
- **Senior Member** — veteran standing: sensitive contracts, high-value relic claims, a voice in hall politics, and the pool the guild draws its officers from.

**Officers** (command authority, promoted from Senior Members)
- **2nd Lieutenant** — junior officer; commands a small crew on contract and holds limited authority over members in the field.
- **1st Lieutenant** — field officer; commands larger expeditions, handles hall placements, and answers for them.
- **Captain** — commands a hall's operations or a standing company; real authority and faction attention.
- **Major** — guild leadership; sets policy for a region and deals with the USBD, the rail, and the Church as a power in their own right.

Standing can be suspended or revoked for murder, theft from a sanctioned site, illegal casting, unpaid debts, contract breach, attacking guild members, selling restricted relics, falsifying reports, or embarrassing powerful patrons.

Separately from rank, the guild and the USBD can issue a **sealed warrant** — a temporary, often deniable authority for a forbidden site, a catastrophic threat, or work no one wants on the books. A sealed warrant is not a rank; an officer of any grade may carry one, and a trusted Senior Member occasionally does.

## 20. Peoples

**Humans and monsters only.** The world was the ordinary Earth until 1850; there are no nonhuman ancestries, elder peoples, or old nonhuman nations. Humanity's diversity is the real 1862's diversity, shaped by the alternate history above. Anything inhuman a person meets is a **monster** of the accepted categories — or a human changed by essence, cultivation, or dungeon phenomena, which is material for play, not a playable ancestry. Essence-touched people (the visibly cultivated, the dungeon-scarred, the altered) may meet curiosity, fear, religious suspicion, USBD interest, or awe depending on person and place; these reactions vary.

## 21. Progression and multiclassing

Mixed progression: characters grow through attribute increases (within the human band by training; past it only by delve payouts on an open core), skills improved by use, talents, archetype abilities, cultivation, magical loot, guild rank, reputation, training, mentors, scars, favors, and discovered knowledge.

Archetypes are starting shapes, not rigid cages — they shape starting abilities, social expectations, and training paths. Multiclassing is allowed through believable access to training, tools, books, sponsorship, mentors, field experience, or supernatural events, and may carry social consequences (a licensed occultist who learns hedge witchcraft draws USBD suspicion; a marshal who learns outlaw methods loses lawful trust). Multiclassing costs time, money, risk, or obligation rather than being a free mechanical choice.

Awakening is a major fork. The moment the character kills any dungeon creature, the core opens — do not spend that beat casually. (For Bill specifically, see `system_engineer_truth_v1.md`.) Magical loot should feel rare, specific, and consequential, not disposable.

Delver archetypes are built as they are encountered in play rather than maintained as a master list. A few touchstones for texture: Gunslinger (revolvers, rifles, intimidation, frontier violence), Monster Hunter (identifying, stalking, trapping, and killing the categories), Licensed Occultist (formally trained, permitted spellcaster under scrutiny), Runecrafter (stable enchantment inscribed into dungeon materials), and Bill's own Engineer (machines, locks, explosives, steamworks, dungeon mechanisms). Build others as the world produces them.

## 22. Reputation and legal status

Track reputation separately across important groups — never one universal score. A character may be respected by the Argent Guild, hated by a rail company, distrusted by a marshal, admired by laborers, and watched by the USBD at once.

Legal status, when relevant: Clean, Watched, Suspected, Wanted locally, Wanted regionally, Guild censured, License suspended, Outlawed.

Law enforcement, guild officers, bounty hunters, newspapers, the Church, rail agents, and town councils react according to what they know, what they can prove, and what benefits them.
